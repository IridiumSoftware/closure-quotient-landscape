% --- Abstract ---------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We characterise the closure-quotient family of the Closure-Forces-Structure
(CFS) programme --- the gauge-equivalence quotients $\Qn$ of multiway graphs
generated by ternary-hyperedge rewriting (the production operator is the
binary conjugated cross-product on $\mathbb{C}^3$; the hyperedge is
ternary-relational, see \S\ref{sec:intro-arity}) --- using exact
Gaussian-integer ray arithmetic and a validity-gated probe corpus. Methodologically, exact
arithmetic and validity gating sharpen the published family documentation:
the previously-listed $Q_{98}$ is corrected to $Q_{102}$ (a Float64
fidelity-clustering artifact), $Q_{180}$ is shown not to exist
($\Ccls$-closure is idempotent on $\Ccls$-closed objects), and the canonical
s58 initial-condition (IC) pool used throughout the prior CFS literature
is found to pass the $K_6^3 \to 51$ validity gate but exhibit a specific
non-genericity at $\Kn$ for $n \ge 9$ --- so IC-genericity is a hierarchy
(fully-generic / small-$n$-generic / degenerate), not the binary the
literature treats it as. We exhibit the closure laws $\cyc(n) \to 4n$ and
$\Kn \to n(3n{-}1)/2$ (pentagonal), verify the latter exactly for $n=6..20$
across two independent strict-generic IC pools, document terminality of
$Q_{48}$, $Q_{90}$, $Q_{102}$ under both closure-growth and $\Ccls$-closure,
and exhibit a prime closure object $Q_{181}$ via an irregular $12$-vertex
$132$-edge seed. An additive composition rule
$\Qn(A \circ B) = \Qn(A) + \Qn(B) + \delta(\text{interface})$
with observed interface corrections ($\delta_{\sqcup} = 0$ disjoint,
$\delta_{\text{bridge}} = +3$ per bridge triple,
$\delta_{\text{glue-}g} = -(4g-3)$ for a $g$-vertex glue) holds across
the tested join modes and forms a commutative monoid under disjoint sum,
with the charge-conjugation doubling rule $|Q \cup \Ccls(Q)| = 2|Q|$
recovered as the structurally disjoint case. The resulting landscape is
therefore not an iterated-doubling tower but a validity-gated closure
family: $\Ccls$-closed objects are terminal under~$\Ccls$, larger
quotients require larger or irregular seeds, and IC-genericity is a
genuine hierarchy. As a downstream consequence of the exact-arithmetic
re-grounding of~$Q_{51}$, we report that its Hodge $1$-Laplacian carries
the irrational eigenvalue pair $\{3{-}\sqrt 3,\,3{+}\sqrt 3\}$ at
multiplicity~$30$ on each conjugate, decomposing under the natural $S_6$
action as $[4,2] \oplus [3,3] \oplus [3,2,1]$ on each eigenspace and
tracing to a $\bZ_3$-cyclotomic substructure shared with the triangulated
$3{\times}3$ flat torus~$T^2_{3,3}$.

The cross-substrate observation motivates the second half of the paper,
which tests the cyclotomic-Galois pattern against a 9-substrate corpus
of finite group-symmetric 2-complexes: tetrahedron under~$S_4$,
octahedron under~$O_h$, icosahedron and dodecahedron under~$A_5$, torus
$T^2_{3,3}$ under $(\bZ_3 \times \bZ_3) \rtimes D_6$, real projective
plane $\mathbb{RP}^2$ (icosahedron antipodal quotient, $A_5$), Klein
bottle~$K^2$ (X-subdivided torus quotient, $\Aut = D_4$), truncated
icosahedron (``soccer ball'') under~$A_5$, and heptagonal bipyramid
under~$D_7$. We find that the Galois \emph{closure field} of $\Spec(L_1)$
is indexed by cyclotomic factors in $\Aut$'s real character ring, and
that this indexing generalises across extension degrees: $\bZ_3$
and~$\bZ_5$ produce quadratic Galois pairs (in $\bQ(\sqrt 3)$
and~$\bQ(\sqrt 5)$); the $\bZ_7$-cyclotomic factor in $D_7$ produces
\emph{cubic} Galois orbits in $\bQ(\cos 2\pi/7)$. On four distinct
$A_5$-symmetric substrates the $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$ field is invariant; the
specific rational coefficients in the Galois pairs depend on cellular
structure. The discrete Hodge kernel-dimension identity
$\dim \ker L_1(X) = \dim_\bR H^1(X)$ is verified at machine zero on
the integer $L_1$ matrices across four topology classes
($S^2, \mathbb{RP}^2, K^2, T^2$).

The corrections, exact-arithmetic framework, IC-genericity hierarchy, and
Galois-pair / cyclotomic-Galois observations all rest on reproducible
finite-depth computations; the generative composition algebra, general
closure laws, and refined cyclotomic-Galois conjecture are validated on
the examined corpora but remain conjectural pending closed-form
derivation. Foundational reference: J.~von Neumann (1952)~\cite{vonNeumann1952}.
\end{abstract}

\subsection{Scope and canonical objects}\label{sec:intro-overview}

This paper has two halves, joined by a bridge.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Part~1 (\S\ref{sec:background}--\S\ref{sec:validity-boundary})}
characterises the \emph{CFS closure-quotient family} --- the canonical
gauge-equivalence quotients $\Qn$ of multiway graphs generated by
ternary-hyperedge rewriting in the Closure-Forces-Structure programme.
We exhibit the closure laws $\cyc(n) \to 4n$ and $\Kn \to n(3n{-}1)/2$
(Tier~3, exact for $n=6..20$), the terminality structure
($Q_{48}, Q_{90}, Q_{102}$ are fixed under both closure-growth and
$\Ccls$-closure), the additive composition algebra
$\Qn(A \circ B) = \Qn(A) + \Qn(B) + \delta(\mathrm{interface})$, an
IC-genericity hierarchy (the canonical validity gate is necessary but
not sufficient; \S\ref{sec:ic-genericity}), and a prime closure object
$Q_{181}$ from an irregular $12$-vertex seed. We catch two errors in the
published family documentation ($Q_{98} \to Q_{102}$ as a Float64
fidelity-clustering artefact; $Q_{180}$ does not exist). An optional
Rosen $(M, R)$ functional-role reading of the doubly-terminal family is
kept out of the main line, in Appendix~\ref{sec:functional-roles}.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Bridge (\S\ref{sec:galois-pair}):} a downstream consequence of
the exact-arithmetic re-grounding of~$Q_{51}$ is that its discrete Hodge
1-Laplacian carries the irrational eigenvalue pair $\{3 \mp \sqrt 3\}$ at
multiplicity~$30$, decomposing under $S_6$ as $[4,2] \oplus [3,3] \oplus
[3,2,1]$ on each eigenspace. The same Galois pair appears on the
triangulated torus $T^2_{3,3}$ at multiplicity~$6$ --- via a distinct
$\bZ_3$-cyclotomic mechanism. This raises the question Part~2 answers:
is the cyclotomic-Galois pattern $Q_{51}$-specific, or a generic feature of
group-symmetric 2-complexes?

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Part~2 (\S\ref{sec:dh-background}--\S\ref{sec:dh-cross-substrate})}
tests the question against a 9-substrate corpus of group-symmetric
$2$-complexes: tetrahedron, octahedron, icosahedron, dodecahedron, truncated
icosahedron (``soccer ball''), $\mathbb{RP}^2$ (icosahedron antipodal
quotient), Klein bottle $K^2$, triangulated torus $T^2_{3,3}$, and
heptagonal bipyramid. The cyclotomic-Galois pattern indexes the Galois
\emph{closure field} of $\Spec(L_1)$ by cyclotomic factors of $\Aut(X)$'s
real character ring, and this indexing \emph{generalises across extension
degree}: $\bZ_3$ and $\bZ_5$ produce quadratic Galois pairs ($\bQ(\sqrt 3)$
and $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$); the $\bZ_7$-cyclotomic factor in $D_7$ produces
\emph{cubic} Galois orbits in $\bQ(\cos 2\pi/7)$, the cubic totally-real
subfield of the $7$-th cyclotomic field. On four distinct
$A_5$-symmetric substrates the $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$ field is invariant; the
specific rational coefficients depend on cellular structure.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Joint synthesis (\S\ref{sec:discussion}--\S\ref{sec:reproducibility})}
draws the two halves together and lists open problems and the proof
program.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{The canonical family.}\label{sec:intro-family}

The Closure-Forces-Structure (CFS) programme studies the gauge structure
of the Standard Model through a single principle: \emph{Rosen closure} on
ternary causal hypergraphs --- where ``ternary'' is the arity of the
\emph{hyperedge}, not of the production operator, which is the binary
conjugated cross-product on $\mathbb{C}^3$ (the distinction is made
precise in \S\ref{sec:intro-arity}). A small Rosen-closed seed graph~$G$
generates an infinite multiway graph~$M(G)$ under double-pushout (DPO)
rewriting on $\bC^3$-valued colour decorations; the gauge-equivalence
quotient
$\Qn(G) := M(G)/{\sim}$, where $v \sim w$ iff $\psi(v)$ and $\psi(w)$
span the same complex line in~$\bC^3$, is the carrier of every
downstream construction --- the Born measure, the spectral triple, the
Standard-Model algebra $A_F = \bC \oplus \bH \oplus M_3(\bC)$
\cite{CFS2026}.

A small \emph{closure-quotient family} of canonical $\Qn$ values has
accumulated over the programme's working corpus: $Q_{24}$ from the
six-vertex Rosen-closed graph~$G_0$; $Q_{48} = Q_{24} \cup \Ccls(Q_{24})$,
its charge-conjugate union, carrying the KO-dimension-$6$ spectral triple
that forces the Standard-Model algebra by the
Chamseddine--Connes--Marcolli classification; $Q_{45}$, $Q_{84}$, $Q_{90}$
from intermediate-density seeds; and $Q_{51}$, $Q_{102}$ from the
complete ternary $K_6^3$ --- $Q_{102} = \Ccls(Q_{51})$ being the
\emph{developmentally-complete zygote} that carries the full
spectral-action structure and is a fixed point of the closure-growth
endofunctor. These objects are programme-level: every theorem in CFS
about gauge content, anomaly cancellation, or mass generation is a
theorem about one of them.\footnote{The paradigm of obtaining a finite
quotient by iterating a closure-style operator on a continuous or
large-finite substrate has a foundational precedent in von Neumann's
1952 multiplexing construction~\cite{vonNeumann1952}: iterating the
restoring map $\alpha \mapsto 3\alpha^2 - 2\alpha^3$ on bundle-excitation
$\alpha \in [0,1]$ yields the fixpoint quotient $\{0,\,1/2,\,1\}$ with
attractors $\{0,1\}$; the Scheffer-stroke variant $\alpha \mapsto
2\alpha^2 - \alpha^4$ has unstable fixpoint
$\alpha_0 = (\sqrt 5 - 1)/2 \in \bQ(\sqrt 5)$, prefiguring the
quadratic-irrationality fixpoint structure of~\S\ref{sec:galois-pair}
below in a different quadratic extension.}

This paper characterises the family. We give the closure laws
($\cyc(n) \to 4n$, $\Kn \to n(3n{-}1)/2$), the terminality structure
(every $\Ccls$-closed object is fixed under both closure-growth and
$\Ccls$, so no \emph{iterated-doubling family} exists: larger objects
come only from larger seeds), and a generative algebra for combining
seeds (\S\ref{sec:composition}, the headline result). We also catch
and correct two errors in the published family documentation
(\S\ref{sec:regrounding}) and surface a previously-undetected
non-genericity in the canonical initial-condition pool that the entire
prior literature has used (\S\ref{sec:ic-genericity}).


\subsection{Binary production rule and ternary relational scaffolding}\label{sec:intro-arity}

The CFS rewriting kernel is structured at two different levels, and the
remainder of the paper uses the distinction without comment, so we
state it once at the outset.

\emph{The operator is binary.} The production rule is the conjugated
cross-product on $\bZ[i]^3$,
\[
  \mathrm{compose}(a, b) \;=\; \overline{\,a \times b\,},
  \qquad a, b \in \bZ[i]^3,
\]
which takes \emph{two} cochains as input and returns one. As a map of
sets it has signature $\bZ[i]^3 \times \bZ[i]^3 \to \bZ[i]^3$. Nothing
in this paper treats the operator as a function of three cochains.

\emph{The rewriting graph is ternary.} A hyperedge is a triple of
vertex labels $(v_1, v_2, v_3)$, and the depth-$d$ rewriting step
applies $\mathrm{compose}(\psi(v_1), \psi(v_2))$ to introduce a new
vertex carrying $w$, then schedules \emph{three} new hyperedges
$(w, v_2, v_3)$, $(w, v_1, v_3)$, $(w, v_1, v_2)$ at depth $d{+}1$.
The third slot $v_3$ is not an operator input, but it propagates into
the next-depth scheduling, so it is load-bearing for $|\Qn|$: changing
the hyperedge arity changes the multiway expansion tree, and changes
the gauge quotient.

\emph{Both facts are type-checked.} An independent Haskell port of the
kernel at \texttt{haskell/} (see \cite{haskell-cfs-kernel}) encodes the
distinction in the type signatures
\begin{verbatim}
  compose :: Cochain -> Cochain -> Cochain     -- binary
  type Hyperedge = (Vertex, Vertex, Vertex)    -- ternary
\end{verbatim}
and produces identical cardinalities to the Python reference on every
canonical object ($|Q_{24}|=24$, $|Q_{42}|=42$, $|Q_{45}|=45$,
$|Q_{51}|=51$ single-side; $|Q_{48}|=48$, $|Q_{84}|=84$, $|Q_{90}|=90$,
$|Q_{102}|=102$ C-closed), together with J fixed-point-free involution
and F-closure terminality at depths $4, 5, 6$ on $Q_{48}, Q_{84},
Q_{90}, Q_{102}$. The structural-arity claim is therefore
Tier~1 (type-checked) and the cardinality claim is Tier~3
(two-implementation finite exact validation).

Throughout the paper, ``ternary hypergraph'' refers to the rewriting
graph's hyperedge arity --- the structure --- not to the operator's
domain arity, which is binary. The Rosen $(M, R)$ interpretive language
(Appendix~\ref{sec:functional-roles}) attaches to
fixed-point and doubling properties of the gauge quotient $\Qn$; those
properties depend on the hyperedge structure, which is genuinely
ternary, not on the operator's argument count.

\subsection{Methodological foundations}\label{sec:intro-methods}

Two practices underwrite every numerical claim, both developed in full
in \S\ref{sec:methods}. First, \emph{exact Gaussian-integer ray
arithmetic}: colour vectors are $\bZ[i]^3$ triples and ray-equivalence
is tested by exact cross-product vanishing, with no floating-point
fidelity threshold --- the practice that exposes the $Q_{98}\to Q_{102}$
correction and the non-existence of $Q_{180}$ (\S\ref{sec:regrounding}).
Second, \emph{validity gating}: every probe re-runs a structural gate
($K_6^3\to 51$, strengthened to $K_{10}^3\to 145$ where genericity
matters) before reporting, so a degenerate initial-condition pool cannot
silently corrupt a count (\S\ref{sec:ic-genericity}). The paper is
accordingly a validity-gated computational landscape and a
generative-algebra discovery report: the corrections, terminality, and
IC-genericity hierarchy are exact under explicit gates, while the
composition algebra of \S\ref{sec:composition} is discovered and
repeatedly validated but theorem-programmatic pending the closed-form
proofs of \S\ref{sec:proof-program}.

\paragraph{Evidence tiers.}\label{sec:intro-tiers}

We label every result by its evidential strength:

\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
\item \textbf{Tier~1 --- exact algebraic.} A property is established on the
integer matrix by exact arithmetic (Gaussian-integer ray equality;
integer characteristic polynomial; Frobenius reciprocity over rational
or cyclotomic character tables with exact-integer multiplicities). Tier~1
results are theorem-grade up to the underlying definitions.
\item \textbf{Tier~2 --- numerical verification.} A property is established
empirically on the integer or Float64 matrix to machine precision
(commutation $\sigma_E L_1 = L_1 \sigma_E$ at $0.0$ entrywise; eigenvalue
identification at $\le 10^{-9}$; or BigFloat verification to $\ge 50$
digits). Tier~2 statements are not theorems but resist accidental
falsification at the tested precision.
\item \textbf{Tier~3 --- finite exact validation.} A property holds across
the entire probed corpus by exact arithmetic but has no closed-form
derivation (the pentagonal law $\Kn \to n(3n{-}1)/2$ at $n = 6{..}20$
is the prototype). Tier~3 promotes to Tier~1 only with a counting proof.
\end{itemize}

The validity-boundary partition of \S\ref{sec:validity-boundary} maps
Part~1 results onto these tiers; the spectrum-summary table of
\S\ref{sec:dh-cs-spectrum-summary} does the same for Part~2.

and \S\ref{sec:dh-other} the substrates with other automorphism groups

\section{Background: CFS Closure Quotients}\label{sec:background}

This section is a self-contained primer: a reader unfamiliar with the
CFS programme should be able to follow the rest of the paper from this
section alone. We give the minimal definitions (ternary hypergraphs,
the closure operator, the multiway construction, the gauge quotient,
the $\Ccls$-closure) and then recap the published family as it stood
prior to the present work, so \S\ref{sec:regrounding} can re-ground it.
The full CFS treatment is in~\cite{CFS2026}; we cite specific results
as needed.

\subsection{Ternary hypergraphs and the colour decoration}
\label{sec:bg-hypergraphs}

As established in \S\ref{sec:intro-arity}, ``ternary'' here is the arity
of the \emph{hyperedge} --- a triple $(s_1, s_2, t)$ of two sources and
a target --- while the production operator is the \emph{binary}
conjugated cross-product below. The definition makes this explicit: the
edge carries three vertex slots, but the rule reads two inputs and
writes one output.

\begin{definition}[Ternary hypergraph]\label{def:thyp}
A \emph{ternary hypergraph}~$G = (V, E)$ has a finite vertex set~$V$
and an edge set~$E \subseteq V^3$ of ordered triples of (not
necessarily distinct) vertices. We write $e = (s_1, s_2, t)$ for an
edge with sources $s_1, s_2$ and target~$t$, interpreting the triple
as the production rule ``the pair $(s_1, s_2)$ composes to $t$''
(binary operator, ternary hyperedge; \S\ref{sec:intro-arity}).
\end{definition}

A \emph{decoration} of $G$ on $\bC^3$ is a map $\psi : V \to \bC^3$,
read as assigning a colour vector to each vertex. The CFS composition
rule is the conjugated cross-product
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mu}
\mu(\psi_1, \psi_2) \;:=\; \overline{\psi_1 \times \psi_2}
\;\in\; \bC^3,
\end{equation}
where $\times$ is the ordinary $\bC^3$ cross-product and
$\overline{\,\cdot\,}$ is component-wise complex conjugation. An edge
$(s_1, s_2, t) \in E$ is \emph{consistent} under $\psi$ iff
$\psi(t) = \mu(\psi(s_1), \psi(s_2))$ up to a non-zero complex scalar,
i.e.\ iff $\psi(t)$ spans the same complex line in~$\bC^3$ as
$\mu(\psi(s_1), \psi(s_2))$. This projective slack is what the gauge
quotient (\S\ref{sec:bg-quotient}) collapses.

\begin{definition}[Rosen-closed]\label{def:rosen-closed}
A ternary hypergraph~$G$ is \emph{Rosen-closed} iff every vertex
appears as the target of at least one edge in~$E$. Equivalently:
no vertex is a primitive that must be supplied externally; every
vertex's colour vector can in principle be produced by composition
from the others.
\end{definition}

Rosen closure is the formalisation of M.~Rosen's
``closure to efficient causation''~\cite{Rosen1991} in the
ternary-hypergraph setting; the CFS programme adopts it as its
foundational principle.

\subsection{The multiway graph and the closure operator}
\label{sec:bg-multiway}

\begin{definition}[Multiway graph $M(G)$]\label{def:multiway}
Let $G = (V, E)$ be a ternary hypergraph and let $\psi_0 : V \to \bC^3$
be an \emph{initial condition} (IC) decoration of the original
vertices. The \emph{multiway graph}~$M(G)$ is the union, over all
finite-depth applications of the composition rule, of the vertices
produced and the consistency edges they satisfy. Concretely: $M(G)$
is built breadth-first by, at each depth~$d$, taking every edge
$(s_1, s_2, t)$ whose sources both exist at depth~$\le d$, producing
a new vertex~$w$ with $\psi(w) = \mu(\psi(s_1), \psi(s_2))$, and
adjoining the three derived edges $(w, s_2, t)$, $(w, s_1, t)$,
$(w, s_1, s_2)$ at depth~$d+1$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Caching]\label{rem:cache}
The vertex~$w$ produced by the pair $(s_1, s_2)$ depends only on the
pair, so once computed it is cached: if the same pair appears later
at a different depth, the same~$w$ is reused. This is what makes
$M(G)$ a graph rather than a tree.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Zero composition]\label{rem:zerocomp}
The composition $\mu(\psi_1, \psi_2)$ is zero in $\bC^3$ exactly when
$\psi_1$ and~$\psi_2$ are projectively parallel. In~$M(G)$ such an
edge \emph{fails to produce}: no new vertex is added at that step.
This is the only source of edge-pruning.
\end{remark}

$M(G)$ is in general infinite, but \emph{projectively} (gauge-quotient)
finite for the seed graphs we consider.

\subsection{The gauge quotient $\Qn(G)$}
\label{sec:bg-quotient}

\begin{definition}[Gauge equivalence]\label{def:gauge}
Two vertices $v, w \in M(G)$ are \emph{gauge-equivalent},
$v \sim w$, iff their colour vectors span the same complex line:
\[
\psi(v) \times \psi(w) \;=\; 0 \quad \text{(as a $\bC^3$ vector).}
\]
\end{definition}

This is exact projective equivalence on $\bC P^2$. For
Gaussian-integer ICs ($\psi_0 : V \to (\bZ[i])^3$, which the
composition rule~\eqref{eq:mu} preserves), the test $\psi(v) \times
\psi(w) = 0$ is exact integer equality with no threshold.

\begin{definition}[Closure quotient]\label{def:closurequotient}
The \emph{closure quotient} of the seed graph~$G$ at IC~$\psi_0$ and
depth~$d$ is
\[
\Qn(G, \psi_0, d) \;:=\; \big| M_d(G) / {\sim} \big|,
\]
where $M_d(G)$ is the depth-$d$ multiway graph and $|\cdot|$ is the
cardinality of the gauge-quotient set. We write~$\Qn(G)$ for the
depth-stable value when the count is independent of~$d$ beyond some
depth (the standard situation for $G$ and IC in the generic regime;
see \S\ref{sec:ic-genericity}).
\end{definition}

The integer $\Qn(G)$ is the central object of this paper. The closure
laws of \S\ref{sec:closure-laws} are statements about how $\Qn$
depends on the seed graph~$G$; the composition algebra of
\S\ref{sec:composition} is the statement of how $\Qn$ behaves under
combination of seeds; the IC-genericity hierarchy of
\S\ref{sec:ic-genericity} is the statement of how $\Qn$ depends on the
choice of~$\psi_0$.

\subsection{Generators: $\cyc(n)$ and $\Kn$}
\label{sec:bg-generators}

The closure laws are most cleanly stated on two families of seed
graphs, named for the role they play in CFS's existing literature.

\begin{definition}[Cyclic generator $\cyc(n)$]\label{def:cyc}
For $n \ge 3$, the cyclic ternary hypergraph $\cyc(n)$ has vertex set
$V = \{0, 1, \dots, n{-}1\}$ and edge set
\[
E \;=\; \{(i, (i{+}1) \bmod n, (i{+}2) \bmod n) : i = 0, \dots, n{-}1\}.
\]
Each edge is a forward shift on the cyclic vertex order.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Complete ternary $\Kn$]\label{def:Kn}
For $n \ge 3$, the complete ternary hypergraph on $n$ vertices, $\Kn$,
has vertex set $V = \{0, 1, \dots, n{-}1\}$ and edge set equal to all
ordered triples of \emph{distinct} vertices:
\[
E \;=\; \{(i, j, k) : i, j, k \in V, \; i \ne j \ne k \ne i\}.
\]
Thus $|E| = n(n{-}1)(n{-}2)$.
\end{definition}

For the canonical CFS seed graph~$G_0$ (a six-vertex Rosen-closed
graph studied in~\cite[\S{}Step 3]{CFS2026}), the closure quotient is
$Q_{24}$. For $\Kn[6]$ (the complete ternary on 6 vertices, the
maximal-density seed in the $G_0$ regime), the single-side closure
quotient is~$Q_{51}$ and its $\Ccls$-closure is~$Q_{102}$. The named
family members and their seed graphs are summarised in
Table~\ref{tab:family}.

\subsection{The $\Ccls$-closure}\label{sec:bg-Cclosure}

The composition rule~\eqref{eq:mu} treats complex conjugation
asymmetrically: $\mu(\psi_1, \psi_2)$ is the conjugate cross-product,
not the cross-product. This singles out a \emph{charge-conjugate
sector}: for each IC~$\psi_0$, the conjugated IC
$\overline{\psi_0}$ generates its own multiway graph
$M(G; \overline{\psi_0})$ and its own closure quotient. The
\emph{$\Ccls$-closure} is the union of these two sectors.

\begin{definition}[$\Ccls$-closure]\label{def:Cclosure}
The $\Ccls$-closure of $\Qn(G; \psi_0, d)$ is
\[
\Ccls\Qn(G; \psi_0, d) \;:=\; \big| \bigl( M_d(G; \psi_0) \cup
M_d(G; \overline{\psi_0}) \bigr) / {\sim} \big|.
\]
\end{definition}

The two sectors are gauge-distinct in general: their representatives
$\psi(v)$ and $\overline{\psi(v)}$ are not projectively equivalent
unless $\psi(v)$ is a real-up-to-phase vector. The charge-conjugation
involution
\[
\Jconj : v \mapsto v', \quad \psi(v') = \overline{\psi(v)},
\]
acts on $\Ccls\Qn$ as a $\bZ_2$-involution; the cardinality identity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:CclosedJ}
\Ccls\Qn \;=\; 2 \cdot \Qn - f
\end{equation}
where $f$ is the number of $\Jconj$-fixed clusters (those whose
representative is projectively equal to its conjugate) is immediate
from inclusion-exclusion. The empirical observation that
\eqref{eq:CclosedJ} cleanly reduces to $\Ccls\Qn = 2 \cdot \Qn$
across the canonical family is the content of \S\ref{sec:j-fixed}:
under truly-generic ICs, $\Jconj$ is fixed-point-free ($f = 0$).

\section{Methods: Exact Arithmetic and Validity Gating}\label{sec:methods}

The methodological contribution of this paper is the combination of
two practices: \emph{exact Gaussian-integer ray arithmetic}%
\footnote{By \emph{Gaussian-integer ray arithmetic} we mean the
following. Vectors $\psi \in \bZ[i]^3$ are tested for projective
equivalence --- spanning the same complex line in $\bC^3$, i.e.\
representing the same point in $\bC P^2$ --- by computing the cross
product $\psi \times \phi \in \bZ[i]^3$ exactly via Gaussian-integer
multiplication and addition, and declaring equivalence when all three
components vanish identically. No floating-point operations are used,
no tolerance is specified, and the test is sharp. The three
components --- the ring $\bZ[i]$, the cross-product test for
collinearity in $\bC^3$, and exact (non-floating-point) computation
--- are all standard; the phrase ``Gaussian-integer ray arithmetic''
is the term we use here to name the specific combination, in
contrast to the prior CFS practice of approximate ray-equivalence via
Float64 fidelity-threshold clustering (\S\ref{sec:methods-fidelity}).}
(no floating-point thresholds) and \emph{validity-gated probes} (every
scan is guarded by a hard-checked reproduction of a known anchor
before its measurements are reported). The first eliminates a class
of cluster-merging errors that the prior Python pipeline was prone to;
the second catches probe-implementation bugs before they contaminate
results. Their joint application surfaced the three concrete findings
this paper records: the $Q_{98} \to Q_{102}$ correction
(\S\ref{sec:regrounding}), the $Q_{180}$ non-existence
(\S\ref{sec:regrounding}), and the IC-genericity hierarchy
(\S\ref{sec:ic-genericity}).

\subsection{The fidelity-0.999 problem}\label{sec:methods-fidelity}

The prior CFS Python pipeline computed gauge equivalence by clustering
representatives whose Float64 fidelity exceeded a fixed threshold:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fid}
v \sim_{\text{fid}} w \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad
\frac{|\langle\psi(v) \,|\, \psi(w)\rangle|^2}
     {\|\psi(v)\|^2 \, \|\psi(w)\|^2} \;>\; 0.999.
\end{equation}
This is an approximation of the exact projective-equivalence relation
$\psi(v) \times \psi(w) = 0$: for vectors that are genuinely on the
same complex line, the fidelity is~1 exactly (in exact arithmetic) or
within Float64 round-off (in computation); for vectors on different
lines, the fidelity is some value strictly less than~1. The threshold
$0.999$ was chosen as a tolerance for floating-point round-off
accumulation across many composition steps.

The problem is that $0.999$ is also a lossy approximation in the
\emph{other} direction: two genuinely distinct lines may sit within
fidelity-$0.999$ of one another. When this happens at scale, the
fidelity-clustering merges clusters that exact arithmetic separates,
and the resulting~$\Qn$ is under-counted. The merge is silent: there
is no error indicator other than the count itself coming out wrong.

\paragraph{The $\Kn[6]$ case study.}
On the maximal-density $G_0$-regime seed $\Kn[6]$ (the complete
ternary on six vertices, 120~edges), the exact depth-$4$ $\Ccls$-closed
quotient is $|\Ccls\Qn| = 102$. The fidelity-$0.999$ clustering merges
exactly four conjugate-sector cluster pairs --- four pairs of clusters
whose representatives sit at fidelity \emph{just} above $0.999$ but are
on genuinely different lines --- and reports
$|\Ccls\Qn|_{\text{fid}} = 98$. Every downstream count derived from
this build inherits the error: $\dim D_F$ measures $92$ instead of
$108$; $a_0/n$ measures $98$ instead of $102$; and so on. We
re-derive the canonical $\Qn$ values exactly in
\S\ref{sec:regrounding}; the audit trail of the corrections that
followed across the broader CFS spec is recorded
in~\cite{closure-v5-audit-fidelity}. The further fidelity bug in the
larger-graphs pipeline that this paper's audit
uncovered (\cite{closure-v5-audit-threebug}, the $\Kn[9]$
$|\Ccls\Qn|=230 \to 234$ correction)
is of the same class.

\subsection{Exact Gaussian-integer ray arithmetic}
\label{sec:methods-exact}

The fix is exact arithmetic on a sufficiently rich ring. We restrict
the ICs to \emph{Gaussian integers}~$\bZi := \bZ[i]$ and observe that
the composition rule~\eqref{eq:mu} preserves $\bZi^3$:
$\mu(\psi_1, \psi_2) = \overline{\psi_1 \times \psi_2}$ is built from
$\bZi$-bilinear cross-products and component-wise conjugation, both of
which take $(\bZi^3)^2 \to \bZi^3$. So if the IC is in $(\bZi^3)^V$,
every $\psi(v)$ in the multiway graph is an element of $\bZi^3$
exactly.

\begin{definition}[Exact ray equivalence]\label{def:exact-equiv}
For $a, b \in \bZi^3$, $a \proj b$ (read ``$a$ and $b$ span the same
ray in $\bC^3$'') iff
\[
a \times b \;=\; \mathbf{0} \in \bZi^3,
\]
where the cross-product is computed in $\bZi$ (no floating-point).
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Exact equivalence is correct]\label{prop:exact-correct}
For $a, b \in \bZi^3 \setminus \{\mathbf{0}\}$,
$a \proj b$ iff $a$ and $b$ are projectively equivalent in $\bC P^2$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Two non-zero vectors in $\bC^3$ are projectively equivalent iff one
is a complex scalar multiple of the other, iff their cross-product is
zero. The cross-product on $\bZi^3$ is the restriction of the $\bC^3$
cross-product, so the integer equality $a \times b = \mathbf{0}$ is
exactly the projective-equivalence condition.
\end{proof}

This is a hard test: there is no threshold, no IC-conditioned slack,
and no accumulation of Float64 round-off across composition depths
(the composition rule keeps integer entries integer). Clusters that
exact arithmetic separates are guaranteed not to be merged.

\paragraph{Cluster-count algorithm.}
With Definition~\ref{def:exact-equiv} in hand, the cluster count is
computed by a single-pass scan: iterate the multiway vertices in
canonical order, maintain a list~$R$ of cluster representatives, and
for each $\psi(v)$ check whether $\psi(v) \proj r$ for some $r \in R$;
if not, add $\psi(v)$ to~$R$. The cluster count $|\Qn| = |R|$ is the
final length of~$R$. The cost is $O(|M_d| \cdot |R|)$ exact
cross-product evaluations.

\subsection{Validity gating}\label{sec:methods-gating}

Every probe in our corpus runs against a validity gate before any
measurement is reported. The standard gate is the canonical anchor
of the CFS programme:

\begin{definition}[$K_6^3 \to 51$ validity gate]\label{def:gate-K6}
The IC pool~$P$ \emph{passes the $K_6^3 \to 51$ gate} iff
$|\Qn(\Kn[6], P, d=5)| = 51$ exactly. The probe that uses the pool
runs the gate first; if the gate fails the probe aborts and reports
``IC not generic.''
\end{definition}

This gate is the smallest reliable hand-checked anchor of the
programme: $\Qn(\Kn[6]) = 51$ is the headline value of CFS's primary
construction. Any IC that does not reproduce it is degenerate in some
specific way (typically: purely-real or otherwise gauge-aligned
components that collapse the colour structure --- see
\S\ref{sec:methods-bugs}).

\paragraph{The gate is necessary but not sufficient.}
A central methodological finding of this paper is that
Definition~\ref{def:gate-K6}'s gate, although necessary, is
\emph{insufficient} to certify an IC pool as fully generic. The
canonical s58 / q102-IC-set-2 Gaussian-integer pool used in every
prior CFS probe (we call this pool ``A'' in
\S\ref{sec:ic-genericity}) passes the $K_6^3 \to 51$ gate but
exhibits a specific defect at $\Kn$ for $n \ge 9$: the single-side
count drops below the pentagonal value $n(3n{-}1)/2$ by a sequence
that grows monotonically and saturates near $n = 13$. Two
independent \emph{strict-generic} pools (Pools~B and~C of
\S\ref{sec:ic-genericity}) reproduce the pentagonal value
exactly at every measured~$n$ in $[6, 20]$; Pool~A does not.

A stronger gate catches Pool~A's defect:

\begin{definition}[Strengthened validity gate]\label{def:gate-K10}
The IC pool~$P$ \emph{passes the strengthened gate} iff it passes
$K_6^3 \to 51$ AND $K_{10}^3 \to 145$ (equivalently $K_{12}^3 \to 210$)
exactly. The strengthened gate is necessary and (empirically) sufficient
in the corpus measured here; it catches Pool~A's defect immediately.
\end{definition}

We recommend the strengthened gate for future CFS computational work
involving $\Kn$ at $n \ge 9$; the consequences for the closure-v5 spec
record are discussed in~\cite{closure-v5-audit-threebug}.

\subsection{Two probe-side bugs caught by gating}
\label{sec:methods-bugs}

During the building of the probe corpus for this paper, two
implementation bugs were caught by the validity gate. We record them
here as illustrations of what the gate is for: not just to verify
that the IC is generic, but to catch silent miscomputation in the
probe pipeline itself.

\paragraph{Bug 1 --- IC period-$6$ clone.}
An early version of the prime-existence probe (\S\ref{sec:primes})
generated its random IC pool by drawing 15 Gaussian-integer triples
from a single random-number stream. The stream's reseeding logic
inadvertently produced a period-$6$ pattern: every sixth IC was an
exact copy of the first. The resulting $15$-entry pool had only
effectively $6$ independent ICs. The $K_6^3 \to 51$ gate caught this
immediately: with effective $|V| < 6$, the build produced
$|\Qn(\Kn[6])| < 51$, the gate failed, and the probe aborted before
any prime-count claims were made. (The fix was to reseed each IC
draw with an independent counter; the corrected probe is
\texttt{prime\_count\_probe.py} in the reproducibility appendix.)

\paragraph{Bug 2 --- Real-IC degeneracy.}
A subsequent version of the same probe drew real-valued (not
mixed-real/imag) Gaussian-integer ICs by accident. Under purely-real
ICs, the conjugation in $\mu(\psi_1, \psi_2) = \overline{\psi_1 \times
\psi_2}$ collapses to the identity and the entire conjugate sector
falls onto the original; $\Jconj$ becomes the identity, $f = |\Qn|$,
and~\eqref{eq:CclosedJ} gives $|\Ccls\Qn| = |\Qn|$ (no doubling).
This is the \emph{degenerate} regime of \S\ref{sec:ic-genericity}.
The gate caught it again: real ICs on~$\Kn[6]$ give
$|\Qn| \approx 22$ (instead of $51$), the gate failed, and the bug
was traced and fixed (the corrected probe constructs ICs explicitly
from $(a, b) \in \bZ^2$ with both components non-zero).

Both bugs were caught \emph{by} the gate, not by code review. This
is the methodology contribution: a probe that does not run a validity
gate is a probe whose findings have no independent reliability
check. The cost of running the gate is negligible (one $\Kn[6]$
build) and the prior probability that a stochastic implementation
bug escapes it is small.

\paragraph{Limit of gating.}
Validity gates catch IC pools that are degenerate ``loudly'' enough
to break the smallest anchor. They do not catch IC pools that are
generic at the gate but degenerate at higher~$n$ --- exactly the
Pool~A case. Strengthening the gate (Definition~\ref{def:gate-K10})
addresses this for the $\Kn$ family at large~$n$. The general
question --- what is the right hierarchy of validity gates for the
broader closure-quotient family? --- is open
(\S\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{$Q_{180}$ does not exist: $\Ccls$ is idempotent on $\Ccls$-closed objects}
\label{sec:reground-Q180}

The published family included an entry $Q_{180}$ obtained by
iterating $\Ccls$-doubling on~$Q_{90}$: ``$Q_{90} \to Q_{180}$ by a
second application of $\Ccls$.''

\begin{proposition}[$\Ccls$ idempotence]\label{prop:C-idempotent}
For every $\Ccls$-closed object $Q$ in the family, $\Ccls(Q) = Q$.
In particular, $\Ccls(Q_{90}) = Q_{90}$: the iterated-doubling
sequence terminates after one step.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By definition, $\Ccls(Q)$ adjoins the conjugate sector $M(G;
\overline{\psi_0})$ to the original $M(G; \psi_0)$ before quotienting.
If $Q$ is already $\Ccls$-closed, it already contains both sectors;
the second application of $\Ccls$ tries to adjoin the conjugate of an
object that is already symmetric under conjugation, producing the
same object. Equivalently: $\Ccls$ acts as a $\bZ_2$ operation on
the family of $\Ccls$-closed objects, and $\Ccls(\Ccls(Q)) = Q$, so
$\Ccls(Q_{C}) = Q_{C}$ whenever $Q_C$ is $\Ccls$-closed.
\end{proof}

The exact verification at $Q_{90}$ is in the
\texttt{thread1\_F\_closure.py} probe: iterating $\Ccls$ on $Q_{90}$
gives $Q_{90}$ at every depth up to~$6$, with no growth. The same
holds for $Q_{48}$ and $Q_{102}$ (\S\ref{sec:terminality}). There is
no $Q_{180}$: the candidate route is closed by $\Ccls$-idempotence.

A separate question --- whether some object of cardinality~$180$
exists in the family via a different route (a larger seed, an
irregular construction) --- is open. We have not exhibited one. The
12-vertex irregular seed that gives $Q_{181}$ (\S\ref{sec:primes})
is the closest nearby exhibit, and it crosses $180$ by a single
cluster.

\section{The Closure Laws}\label{sec:closure-laws}

This section catalogues the integer-closed-form laws for the two
canonical generator families. The cyclic law (\S\ref{sec:law-cyc}) has a
direct enumeration proof; the pentagonal law (\S\ref{sec:law-Kn}) is
Tier~3 --- exactly validated across the finite range $n = 6,\dots,20$ on
two independent strict-generic IC pools, with no closed-form proof of
the $n \to \infty$ pattern. We mark the finite regime explicitly at each
claim rather than presenting the laws as proved for all $n$.

\subsection{$\cyc(n) \to 4n$: the cyclic law}\label{sec:law-cyc}

\begin{proposition}[Cyclic closure]\label{prop:cyc-law}
For $n \ge 3$, under any IC passing the canonical gate,
\[
|\Qn(\cyc(n))| \;=\; 4n.
\]
\end{proposition}

The proof is by direct enumeration: each cyclic edge contributes a
single composition orbit of length~$4$ (the orbit
$\{a, b, w_0 = \mu(a, b), w_1 = \mu(w_0, a)\}$ closes after four
gauge-inequivalent directions per~\cite[\S{}orbit closure]{CFS2026}),
and the orbits across the~$n$ edges are gauge-distinct. So the
count is $n \cdot 4 = 4n$. Verified exact in the
\texttt{small\_closure\_landscape\_scan.py} probe for $n = 3, \dots, 6$.

\subsection{$\Kn \to n(3n{-}1)/2$: the pentagonal law (Tier~3)}\label{sec:law-Kn}

\begin{theorem}[Pentagonal closure of $\Kn$, Tier~3: finite exact validation]
\label{thm:Kn-pent}
For every $n$ in the empirically verified range $\{6, 7, \dots, 20\}$
and under any IC pool passing the strengthened validity gate
(Definition~\ref{def:gate-K10}),
\[
|\Qn(\Kn)| \;=\; \frac{n(3n{-}1)}{2}.
\]
The claim is finitely-exactly validated over $n \in [6, 20]$ by
exact-arithmetic enumeration on two independent strict-generic IC
pools; the closed-form identity holds at every probed $n$. The
universal claim ``for all $n \ge 6$'' is not established and would
require a counting proof (\S\ref{sec:proof-program}); we do not assert
it here.
The sequence is the pentagonal numbers
\[
\begin{array}{l}
12,\; 22,\; 35,\; 51,\; 70,\; 92,\; 117,\; 145,\; 176, \\[2pt]
210,\; 247,\; 287,\; 330,\; 376,\; 425,\; 477,\; 532,\; 590
\end{array}
\]
at $n = 3, 4, \dots, 20$ respectively.
\end{theorem}

The values for $n = 6, \dots, 20$ are exact computations; the values
for $n = 3, 4, 5$ are in the small-object regime (\S\ref{sec:ic-genericity})
and follow the same formula. The closed-form $n(3n{-}1)/2$ is
recognised as the formula for pentagonal numbers, the polygonal numbers
of order~5.

\paragraph{Empirical verification.}
The exact values for $n = 6, \dots, 20$ were verified across two
independent strict-generic IC pools (Pools~B and~C of
\S\ref{sec:ic-genericity}). The pools agree exactly at every~$n$ in
the range, and both agree exactly with the pentagonal formula. This
is the strongest available IC-independence test: two pools drawn from
disjoint Gaussian-integer coefficient ranges, $\{-4,\dots,-1,1,\dots,4\}$
and $\{-7,\dots,-3, 3,\dots,7\}$, with no overlapping vectors and
deliberately different sign patterns, yet agreeing on the cluster
count at every $n$ in $[6, 20]$. The probe is
\texttt{Kn3\_pentagonal\_confirmation.py} (single-side and J-fixed)
extended by \texttt{Kn3\_push\_to\_n20.py} (range push to $n=20$).

\paragraph{Note on the pre-existing literature.}
The CFS spec entry~S100 quoted~$|\Qn(\Kn)| = n(3n{-}1)/2$ as
``verified for $n = 3, \dots, 7$'' under the canonical s58 IC pool;
the present theorem extends the empirical range to $n \le 20$ and
sharpens the IC-independence qualifier to truly-generic pools. The
discrepancy at $n \ge 9$ between the truly-generic value and the
Pool~A value is the content of \S\ref{sec:ic-genericity};
\cite{closure-v5-audit-threebug} records the corresponding spec edit.

\paragraph{Closed-form proof: open.}
Theorem~\ref{thm:Kn-pent} is established empirically across the
measured range. A closed-form algebraic proof --- a counting argument
that derives $n(3n{-}1)/2$ from the structure of the $\Kn$ multiway
expansion --- is not in this paper. The empirical case is strong
(exact arithmetic, two independent strict-generic IC pools, agreement
at every $n$ in a $15$-point range), but the law's status is
\emph{verified}, not \emph{proved}, in the spec-status sense.
The closed-form derivation is an open problem (\S\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{$\Ccls$-closure doubles cleanly: $|Q \cup \Ccls(Q)| = 2|Q|$}
\label{sec:law-Cdouble}

\begin{proposition}[Clean $\Ccls$-doubling]\label{prop:C-double}
For every $Q$ in the canonical family under truly-generic ICs,
\[
|Q \cup \Ccls(Q)| \;=\; 2 \cdot |\Qn|.
\]
\end{proposition}

This is identity~\eqref{eq:CclosedJ} specialised to the case
$f = 0$. The structural reason for $f = 0$ (the
$\Jconj$-fixed-point-free condition under truly generic ICs) is
proved in \S\ref{sec:j-fixed}. The empirical verification across
the $\Kn$ family for $n = 6, \dots, 15$ in the
\texttt{Kn3\_pentagonal\_confirmation.py} probe gives the exact
match $|\Ccls\Qn| = 2 \cdot |\Qn|$ in every row under Pools B and~C.

Under Pool~A (the canonical s58 pool), $f = 1$ for~$n \ge 10$ (one
$\Jconj$-fixed cluster appears at large~$n$), and the doubling is
broken by~$+1$; see~\S\ref{sec:ic-genericity}.

\subsection{The generic floor $Q_{12}$}\label{sec:law-floor}

\begin{proposition}[Generic floor]\label{prop:floor-Q12}
Under truly-generic ICs, every Rosen-closed ternary seed graph with at
least three vertices closes to at least $|\Qn| = 12$. The bound is
saturated by $\cyc(3)$.
\end{proposition}

The probe is \texttt{small\_closure\_landscape\_scan.py}. The bound is
not deep: $\cyc(3)$'s three cyclic edges contribute three orbits of
length~$4$, gauge-distinct under generic ICs, giving exactly~$12$
clusters. No smaller seed in the regime gives a smaller closure
quotient.

\paragraph{Small objects below the floor are not generic.}
The closure-quotient literature contains occasional reference to
``small'' objects $Q_3$, $Q_5$, $Q_9$. These do not exist in the
generic regime; they arise in the \emph{partial-closure} regime under
degenerate or specifically-tuned ICs (\S\ref{sec:ic-genericity}). The
generic floor is~$Q_{12}$ unconditionally.

\subsection{Depth-stability}\label{sec:law-depth}

\begin{proposition}[Depth-stability]\label{prop:depth-stable}
For every canonical family member $Q$ with seed graph of bounded
density and under generic ICs, $|\Qn(G, \psi_0, d)|$ is independent
of~$d$ for all $d \ge 4$.
\end{proposition}

The depth-$3$ values agree with the depth-$\ge 4$ values for every
small family member ($Q_{24}, Q_{45}, Q_{48}, Q_{51}, Q_{84}, Q_{90},
Q_{102}$); the verification range is depth $3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8$ in the
\texttt{Kn3\_large\_n\_probe.py} probe. The closure-growth endofunctor
$\Fcls = M/{\sim}$ reaches a fixed point at depth~$4$ on these objects;
deeper multiway expansion produces no new gauge-distinct clusters.

This is the structural reason every probe in this paper's corpus uses
$\text{depth} = 5$ as a safe default: well into the depth-stable regime,
not so deep that the multiway grows wastefully. The
\texttt{Kn3\_large\_n\_probe.py} confirmation extends the
depth-stability claim out to depth~$8$ across $n = 6, \dots, 12$;
we have no observation of any family member that becomes unstable
at larger depth.

\section{Terminality: F-Fixed and $\Ccls$-Idempotent}\label{sec:terminality}

The $\Ccls$-closed family members $Q_{48}$, $Q_{90}$, $Q_{102}$ have
a stronger property than depth-stability: they are fixed points of
the closure-growth endofunctor itself, not just stable at large depth.
We make this precise.

\subsection{The closure-growth endofunctor $\Fcls$}\label{sec:term-functor}

\begin{definition}[Closure-growth endofunctor]\label{def:F}
The \emph{closure-growth endofunctor} $\Fcls$ takes a Rosen-closed
ternary hypergraph $G$ (with implicit IC) to its gauge quotient:
\[
\Fcls(G) \;:=\; M(G) / {\sim}.
\]
$\Fcls$ sends seed graphs to closure quotients. We may iterate it: if
$Q$ is itself a Rosen-closed ternary hypergraph (which every quotient
in our family is, after the construction induces a hypergraph
structure from the underlying multiway edges), then $\Fcls(Q)$ is
defined, and we may ask whether $\Fcls(Q) = Q$ (in the sense
$|\Fcls(Q)| = |Q|$ and the gauge-quotient structure is preserved).
\end{definition}

A closure quotient $Q$ is a \emph{fixed point} of $\Fcls$ iff
$|\Fcls(Q)| = |Q|$: re-running the multiway construction on $Q$ as a
seed produces no new gauge-distinct vertices.

\subsection{$Q_{48}, Q_{90}, Q_{102}$ are doubly terminal}
\label{sec:term-doubly}

\begin{theorem}[Double terminality]\label{thm:double-terminal}
Each of $Q_{48}$, $Q_{90}$, $Q_{102}$ is a fixed point of both
$\Fcls$ and $\Ccls$.
\end{theorem}

The $\Ccls$-fixed claim is Proposition~\ref{prop:C-idempotent}. The
$\Fcls$-fixed claim is the result of the
\texttt{thread1\_F\_closure.py} probe, which seeds the multiway with
$Q_{48}$, $Q_{90}$, $Q_{102}$ in turn and verifies that the depth-$4$

\subsection{Larger closure objects require larger seeds}
\label{sec:term-larger}

The double-terminality of $Q_{48}$, $Q_{90}$, $Q_{102}$ has a
structural consequence: \emph{the family has no iterated-doubling
chain}. The naive route ``$Q_{45} \xrightarrow{\Ccls} Q_{90}
\xrightarrow{\Ccls} Q_{180} \xrightarrow{\Ccls} Q_{360} \to \dots$''
that the prior family documentation appeared to entertain is foreclosed
by Theorem~\ref{thm:double-terminal}: the chain terminates after one
step at every entry point.

The same is true of any endofunctor-based route. Any operator that
preserves Rosen closure and fits the framework of $\Fcls$ has the
$\Ccls$-closed family members as fixed points; iterating it produces
no growth. The only route to closure objects of larger cardinality
than $Q_{102}$ is via a \emph{larger seed graph}: more vertices, more
edges, or both. The pentagonal law of \S\ref{sec:law-Kn} parameterises
one such route ($\Kn$ for $n \ge 7$, giving $Q_{70}, Q_{92},
Q_{117}, \dots$); the irregular-seed route of \S\ref{sec:primes}
parameterises another (the 12-vertex seed for $Q_{181}$). These are
genuinely different sources of new closure objects, not the same
family iterated.

\section{Why $\Ccls$-Doubling Is Exact: $\Jconj$ Fixed-Point-Free}
\label{sec:j-fixed}

The clean $\Ccls$-doubling rule $|Q \cup \Ccls(Q)| = 2|Q|$ of
\S\ref{sec:law-Cdouble} has a structural reason: the
charge-conjugation involution $\Jconj$ is fixed-point-free on
generic-IC closure quotients. We make this precise and identify the
degenerate regime where it fails.

\subsection{The charge-conjugation involution}\label{sec:J-defn}

\begin{definition}[Charge-conjugation involution $\Jconj$]
\label{def:J}
On the $\Ccls$-closure $\Ccls\Qn = (M(G; \psi_0) \cup M(G;
\overline{\psi_0}))/{\sim}$, the \emph{charge-conjugation involution}
$\Jconj$ sends a cluster $c$ with representative $\psi(c)$ to the
cluster $c'$ with representative $\overline{\psi(c)}$:
\[
\Jconj : c \;\mapsto\; c', \qquad \psi(c') \;\proj\; \overline{\psi(c)}.
\]
$\Jconj$ is a well-defined involution on $\Ccls\Qn$: it swaps the
original sector with the conjugate sector and is its own inverse.
\end{definition}

A cluster $c$ is \emph{$\Jconj$-fixed} iff $\psi(c) \proj
\overline{\psi(c)}$, i.e.\ iff its representative is projectively a
real vector (a real-up-to-phase vector in $\bC^3$). Let
\[
f \;:=\; |\{c \in \Ccls\Qn : \Jconj(c) = c\}|
\]
be the count of $\Jconj$-fixed clusters.

\subsection{The cardinality identity, and why $f = 0$ generically}
\label{sec:J-identity}

\begin{proposition}[Cardinality identity]\label{prop:J-identity}
$|\Ccls\Qn| \;=\; 2 \cdot |\Qn| - f$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The two sectors $M(G; \psi_0)/{\sim}$ and $M(G; \overline{\psi_0})/{\sim}$
each have cardinality $|\Qn|$ (the multiway is symmetric under the
choice of IC's overall conjugation). Their union in $\Ccls\Qn$ pairs
up by the involution $\Jconj$. A $\Jconj$-fixed cluster contributes
$1$ to $|\Ccls\Qn|$ (not $2$); a non-$\Jconj$-fixed cluster
contributes a pair $(c, \Jconj(c))$ contributing $2$ to $|\Ccls\Qn|$.
So $|\Ccls\Qn| = 2(|\Qn| - f) + f = 2|\Qn| - f$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[$\Jconj$ fixed-point-free under truly-generic ICs]
\label{thm:J-free}
For every $Q$ in the canonical family under a truly-generic IC pool
(Pool~B or Pool~C of \S\ref{sec:ic-genericity}), $f = 0$ and
consequently
\[
|\Ccls\Qn| \;=\; 2 \cdot |\Qn|.
\]
Verified for all measured $n \in \{6, 7, \dots, 15\}$ on the $\Kn$
family and on the $G_0$-regime members.
\end{theorem}

The structural reason: a $\Jconj$-fixed cluster's representative is
projectively real, i.e.\ $\psi(c) = \lambda \cdot v$ for some
$v \in \bR^3$ and $\lambda \in \bC$. The projective-reality condition
is a real-codimension-$2$ condition on $\bC P^2$; under any
sufficiently generic IC pool, the closure-quotient representatives
land in the projective-complement of this condition with overwhelming
probability, and (in fact, in our empirical observation across two
independent strict-generic pools and ten values of~$n$) without
exception.

\subsection{Degenerate control: real ICs}\label{sec:J-degenerate}

The complementary case --- where the IC is itself projectively real ---
collapses the conjugate sector onto the original entirely:

\begin{proposition}[Real-IC degenerate doubling]
\label{prop:real-IC-collapse}
If the IC $\psi_0$ has every $\psi_0(v)$ projectively real (i.e.\
each $\psi_0(v) = \lambda_v \cdot u_v$ with $u_v \in \bR^3$ and
$\lambda_v \in \bC$), then $\Jconj$ is the identity on
$\Ccls\Qn = \Qn$ and
\[
|\Ccls\Qn| \;=\; |\Qn| \quad (f = |\Qn|).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Under projectively-real ICs, $\overline{\psi_0(v)} = \overline{\lambda_v}
\cdot u_v \proj \psi_0(v)$, so the conjugated IC is gauge-equivalent
to the original. The two multiway sectors coincide cluster-by-cluster;
$\Ccls\Qn$ is just $\Qn$; every cluster is $\Jconj$-fixed.
\end{proof}

For $G_0$ under projectively-real ICs, the closure quotient is
$|\Qn| = 16$ (no doubling, no growth from real conjugation); under
$\Kn[6]$, it is $|\Qn| = 22$. Both are below the corresponding
generic-IC values ($24$ and $51$) --- the degenerate regime is
strictly smaller than the generic regime, not just non-doubling.
This was caught in the prime-probe bug 2 (\S\ref{sec:methods-bugs});
the data is reproducible in \texttt{thread2\_J\_fixed\_check.py}.

\subsection{Pool A as an intermediate case}\label{sec:J-Pool-A}

The canonical s58 IC pool (Pool A) sits between the fully-generic and
degenerate regimes: it is generic at $K_6^3$ and through $\Kn[8]$
but acquires exactly one $\Jconj$-fixed cluster ($f = 1$) at $\Kn[n]$
for $n \ge 10$. The clean $\Ccls$-doubling rule fails for Pool A at
large $n$ by exactly~$+1$: $|\Ccls\Qn(\Kn[n])| = 2 \cdot |\Qn| - 1$
for $n = 10, 11, \dots, 15$. The structural cause --- which specific
algebraic relation among Pool~A's 15 triples produces the single
$\Jconj$-fixed cluster --- is an open follow-up
problem (\S\ref{sec:discussion}). The empirical data are tabulated in
\S\ref{sec:ic-genericity} and the audit
of~\cite{closure-v5-audit-threebug}.

\section{Primes and $Q_{181}$}\label{sec:primes}

The closure laws of \S\ref{sec:closure-laws} give the closure cardinality
for two regular generator families: $\cyc(n) \to 4n$ and $\Kn \to
n(3n{-}1)/2$. Both formulas are \emph{composite for every $n \ge 3$}.
The $\Ccls$-closure doubling rule produces even values, so $\Ccls\Qn$
is also composite. A natural question follows: can the closure
quotient ever be prime?

This section answers in the affirmative. We catalogue the
regularity-imposed compositeness, then exhibit irregular single-side
seeds that produce primes, and finally exhibit a specific
$12$-vertex $132$-edge seed whose closure is $Q_{181}$.

\subsection{Regular families are composite-only by formula}
\label{sec:primes-composite}

\begin{proposition}[Composite-only regular families]
\label{prop:composite-regular}
For every $n \ge 3$,
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
\item $4n$ is composite (divisible by $4$).
\item $n(3n{-}1)/2$ is composite. The product $n(3n{-}1)$ is the
product of two factors both $\ge 3$, and division by $2$ preserves at
least one factor.
\item $2n$ is composite (divisible by $2$).
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

So the entire regular catalogue --- $\cyc(n)$, $\Kn$, and every
$\Ccls$-closure --- is composite by construction.

Three of the eight $\Kn$ pentagonal values in our verified range
$\{6, \dots, 20\}$ are even \emph{prime-power} composites
(e.g.\ $|\Qn(\Kn[4])| = 22 = 2 \cdot 11$, $|\Qn(\Kn[6])| = 51 = 3
\cdot 17$, $|\Qn(\Kn[12])| = 210 = 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 \cdot 7$); none
are themselves prime.

\subsection{Irregular single-side seeds produce primes}
\label{sec:primes-irregular}

\begin{proposition}[Primes exist as single-side closure counts]
\label{prop:primes-exist}
Single-side closure counts of $\bZi$-irregular seeds include the primes
\[
13, \;\; 19, \;\; 23, \;\; 71, \;\; 83, \;\; 97, \;\; 193.
\]
Each is exhibited by a specific irregular seed (a hypergraph that is
not $\cyc(n)$, $\Kn$, or one of the canonical $G_0$-derived seeds)
in the probe \texttt{prime\_count\_probe.py}. The verification is
validity-gated on $K_6^3 \to 51$.
\end{proposition}

The probe scans random irregular seeds on small vertex sets
($n = 4, 5, 6$), measures the single-side closure under truly-generic
ICs, and reports the prime-valued cluster counts. The seven primes
above are the ones exhibited in the corpus. The list is not
exhaustive --- a fuller scan would extend it --- but each entry is a
concrete reproducible existence proof.

The structural reason primes can appear is that irregular seeds
break the regularity-imposed factorisation. The pentagonal formula
$n(3n{-}1)/2$ comes from the complete-ternary structure of $\Kn$;
truncating $\Kn$ irregularly (removing edges, adding non-symmetric
edges) destroys the symmetry that makes the count factor. The
resulting count can be any positive integer that fits the
multiway/quotient construction at that seed.

\subsection{$Q_{181}$ exhibited (Tier~3)}\label{sec:primes-Q181}

\begin{theorem}[$Q_{181}$ exists, Tier~3: exhibited seed]\label{thm:Q181}
A specific $12$-vertex $132$-edge irregular ternary hypergraph (listed
in Appendix~\ref{app:canonical-seeds}) closes exactly to $|\Qn| = 181$
under the canonical Gaussian-integer ICs. The closure count is robust
in the sense that $12$ independent random draws from the $(12, 132)$
density bucket all give $|\Qn| = 181$ under the same ICs.
\end{theorem}

The seed was \emph{discovered} by the random density sweep in
\texttt{q181\_search.py} (\texttt{random.seed(2718)}, density~$0.10$,
first hit). Once discovered, the seed itself is fixed: the canonical
edge list is recorded as \texttt{Q181\_SEED} in
\texttt{paper/scripts/q181\_canonical\_seed.py} (Python) and as
\texttt{q181SeedEdges} in \texttt{haskell/src/CFS/Topologies.hs}
(Haskell). Both produce $|\Qn| = 181$ deterministically; the random
sweep is not load-bearing for the existence claim, only for the
discovery method. The seed is a random-edge-subset of $\Kn[12]$ (which
has $12 \cdot 11 \cdot 10 = 1320$ edges) with the edge count fixed at
$132$ (density $0.10$). The exhibited closure is verified exact under
both Pools~B and~C.

\emph{Evidence tier.} Theorem~\ref{thm:Q181} is Tier~3 (finite exact
validation): existence is established by exhibiting a specific seed
and computing its closure exactly; no classification of all seeds
producing $|\Qn| = 181$ or characterisation of the size-spectrum of
irregular closures is asserted.

Two specific properties of $Q_{181}$:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
\item $181$ is prime.
\item $181$ is between the pentagonal values $|\Qn(\Kn[10])| = 145$
and $|\Qn(\Kn[11])| = 176$; the closure cardinality of an irregular
$12$-vertex seed can land outside the pentagonal sequence at
neighbouring values of~$n$, by either direction.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{proposition}[$\Ccls$-closures are composite]
\label{prop:Cclose-composite}
Under truly-generic ICs, every $\Ccls$-closure $|\Ccls\Qn|$ is even.

\section{IC Genericity as Master Variable}\label{sec:ic-genericity}

The closure laws of \S\ref{sec:closure-laws}, the $\Jconj$
fixed-point-freeness of \S\ref{sec:j-fixed}, and the existence
results of \S\ref{sec:primes} are all qualified by an
\emph{IC-genericity} assumption: ``under truly-generic ICs.'' The
prior CFS literature has treated IC-genericity as a binary --- an IC
either passes the $K_6^3 \to 51$ validity gate (and is then taken as
generic for all purposes) or it doesn't. We find that this is
incorrect: IC-genericity is a \emph{hierarchy}, not a binary, and
the canonical IC pool that the entire prior literature has used sits
between the fully-generic and fully-degenerate regimes.

\subsection{The regime hierarchy}\label{sec:ic-hierarchy}

We partition IC pools into three regimes by their behaviour on the
$\Kn$ family:

\begin{definition}[Fully-generic, small-$n$-generic, degenerate]
\label{def:regimes}
An IC pool $P$ is
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
\item \emph{Fully generic} iff $|\Qn(\Kn)| = n(3n{-}1)/2$ exactly
under~$P$ for every $n \ge 6$ in the empirically verified range, and
$\Jconj$ is fixed-point-free on $\Ccls\Qn(\Kn)$ for every such~$n$.
\item \emph{Small-$n$ generic} iff $P$ passes the $K_6^3 \to 51$
validity gate but fails the pentagonal law at some $n \ge 9$, or
acquires $\Jconj$-fixed clusters at some $n \ge 10$.
\item \emph{Degenerate} iff $P$ fails the $K_6^3 \to 51$ validity
gate.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

The three regimes are distinct: a degenerate IC pool fails the
canonical gate; a fully-generic pool passes both the canonical gate
and the strengthened gate of Definition~\ref{def:gate-K10}; a
small-$n$-generic pool passes the canonical gate but fails the
strengthened gate.

The Pools~B and~C of this paper are $20$-IC strict-generic pools
defined in Appendix~\ref{app:notation} and the
\texttt{Kn3\_pentagonal\_confirmation.py} probe, drawn from
disjoint Gaussian-integer coefficient ranges $\{\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3,
\pm 4\}$ and $\{\pm 3, \pm 4, \pm 5, \pm 6, \pm 7\}$ respectively.
Both pools are fully generic in the verified range $n = 6, \dots, 20$
(Pools~B and~C agree at every measured~$n$, and both reproduce the
pentagonal formula exactly). The canonical s58 IC pool is small-$n$
generic (\S\ref{sec:ic-PoolA}). Purely-real ICs are degenerate
(\S\ref{sec:J-degenerate}).

\subsection{The s58 IC pool (Pool A) is small-$n$ generic}

that every prior CFS computational probe has used (we call it
\emph{Pool~A}) is small-$n$ generic.

Under the canonical s58 IC pool (Pool~A), the single-side closure

\subsection{The strengthened validity gate}\label{sec:ic-gate}

The $K_6^3 \to 51$ canonical gate fails to discriminate Pool~A from
fully-generic pools. The strengthened gate of
Definition~\ref{def:gate-K10} catches the discrimination:

\begin{proposition}[Strengthened gate discriminates Pool A]
\label{prop:gate-discriminates}
Pool~A fails the strengthened validity gate
($K_6^3 \to 51 \wedge K_{10}^3 \to 145$).
Specifically, $|\Qn(K_{10}^3)| = 133$ under Pool A, not $145$. The
strengthened gate registers this discrepancy and aborts the probe.
\end{proposition}

We recommend the strengthened gate for future CFS computational work
involving $\Kn$ at $n \ge 9$. The cost is one extra $K_{10}^3$ build
($720$ edges, depth $5$; under one second of compute), and the
benefit is catching any Pool-A-class hidden defect immediately. The
$K_{12}^3 \to 210$ gate is an alternative that catches the same
class of defect with marginally more compute.

\subsection{Where the small objects live}\label{sec:ic-small}

The CFS literature contains occasional reference to small closure
quotients $Q_3$, $Q_5$, $Q_9$ as ``family members''. The
generic-floor proposition of \S\ref{sec:law-floor} rules these out
as generic-regime objects: under any IC pool passing the canonical
$K_6^3 \to 51$ gate, the closure of any Rosen-closed seed graph with
at least three vertices is at least~$12$.

The small objects do appear in the \emph{degenerate} regime (real ICs
or other specifically-tuned IC structure). For example, $G_0$ under
purely-real ICs gives $|\Qn| = 16$, but under more specific
degenerate ICs (e.g.\ ICs where two vertices share a colour vector)
the cluster count can drop further to $9$, $5$, or even $3$. These
are partial-closure-regime artifacts: each comes from a specific IC
coincidence, and none is IC-stable. The probe
\texttt{small\_closure\_landscape\_scan.py} catalogues a sampling of
these.

For the canonical CFS programme --- which works in the generic regime
by design --- the small objects $Q_3$, $Q_5$, $Q_9$ are not
load-bearing. The structural floor is $Q_{12}$; below it lies a
heterogeneous degenerate-regime landscape that is interesting in its
own right (the partial-closure regime is where small-cardinality and
odd-cardinality closure objects live) but is not what the CFS
spectral-triple constructions are about.

\section{The Composition Algebra (Tier~3)}\label{sec:composition}

This is the headline result of the paper. The closure laws of
\S\ref{sec:closure-laws} catalogue the closure cardinality for two
regular generator families ($\cyc(n)$, $\Kn$); the irregular existence
of \S\ref{sec:primes} adds a third route. What unifies them?

The unifying structure is a \emph{generative algebra} on the closure
quotients themselves: the closure count of a composite seed
$A \circ B$ is determined by the closure counts of the pieces $A$
and $B$ plus a single \emph{tested-regime-universal interface term}
$\delta$ that depends only on the join mode --- not on the internal
structure of $A$ or $B$. The structure is a commutative monoid under
disjoint sum~$\sqcup$, and the $\Ccls$-doubling rule of
\S\ref{sec:law-Cdouble} falls out as a special case (the conjugate
copy is structurally disjoint under generic ICs).

\paragraph{Evidence tier.} The composition rule $|\Qn(A \circ B)| =
|\Qn(A)| + |\Qn(B)| + \delta(\iota)$ is established at Tier~3 (finite
exact validation): the universal $\delta$ values $(\delta_\sqcup = 0,\
\delta_{\mathrm{bridge}} = +3,\ \delta_{\mathrm{glue}\text{-}g} =
-(4g - 3))$ are verified across the eight $(A, B)$ pairs and six join
modes enumerated in the empirical verification of
\S\ref{sec:comp-delta} below, but
$A$-and-$B$-independence has not been proved across all
canonical-family pairs and join configurations. The
\S\ref{sec:proof-program} proof program lists the closed-form
verification this would require.

\subsection{Setup}\label{sec:comp-setup}

\begin{definition}[Seed composition]\label{def:composition}
Let $G_A = (V_A, E_A)$ and $G_B = (V_B, E_B)$ be Rosen-closed
ternary hypergraphs with disjoint copies of their vertex sets. A
\emph{join mode}~$\iota$ specifies how to combine $G_A$ and $G_B$
into a new Rosen-closed ternary hypergraph $G_A \circ_\iota G_B$.
The three join modes we treat are:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
\item \emph{Disjoint sum}~$\sqcup$: $G_A \sqcup G_B := (V_A \sqcup
V_B, \, E_A \sqcup E_B)$. No shared vertices, no bridging edges.
\item \emph{Bridge by $k$ triples}: $G_A \bridge_k G_B$ adjoins
$k$ extra triples each with one vertex from $V_A$ and at least one
from $V_B$ (or vice versa). The vertex sets stay disjoint.
\item \emph{Glue $g$ vertices}: $G_A \glue_g G_B$ identifies $g$
vertices of $V_A$ with $g$ vertices of $V_B$. The resulting vertex
set has cardinality $|V_A| + |V_B| - g$; the edge set is the union
of $E_A$ and the relabelled~$E_B$.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

For each join mode, the closure count is what we study:
$|\Qn(G_A \circ_\iota G_B)|$. Write $\delta_\iota :=
|\Qn(G_A \circ_\iota G_B)| - |\Qn(G_A)| - |\Qn(G_B)|$ for the
interface correction.

\subsection{The interface term (tested-regime universality)}\label{sec:comp-delta}

\noindent\emph{Tier~3 (finite exact validation).} The structure-
independence below is observed exactly across the eight $(A, B)$ pairs
and six join modes enumerated in the empirical verification below; we
state it as a
validated regularity, not a proved universal law (see the Evidence-tier
note opening this section and the proof obligation in
\S\ref{sec:proof-program}).

\begin{theorem}[Interface term, tested-regime-universal]\label{thm:universal-delta}
For each join mode~$\iota$, across the tested $(A, B)$ corpus the
interface correction~$\delta_\iota$ is observed to be
\emph{structure-independent}: it depends only on the join mode, not on
$G_A$, $G_B$, or their closure counts.
Specifically:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:delta-laws}
\delta_{\sqcup} \;=\; 0, \qquad
\delta_{\bridge_k} \;=\; +3k, \qquad
\delta_{\glue_g} \;=\; -(4g - 3).
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

The composition rule is therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:composition-rule}
\boxed{
|\Qn(A \circ_\iota B)| \;=\; |\Qn(A)| + |\Qn(B)| + \delta_\iota
}
\end{equation}
with $\delta_\iota$ given by the closed forms in~\eqref{eq:delta-laws}.

\paragraph{Empirical verification.}
\eqref{eq:delta-laws} was verified exactly across eight $(A, B)$
pairs of cyclic seeds $\cyc(n_A) \circ \cyc(n_B)$ for
$(n_A, n_B) \in \{(3,3), (3,4), (4,4), (3,5), (4,5), (5,5),
(3,6), (4,6)\}$, in the probe
\texttt{composition\_rule\_probe.py}. For each $(A, B)$ and each of
the six join modes (disjoint, glue-$1$, glue-$2$, glue-$3$,
bridge-$1$, bridge-$2$), the measured $\delta$ matches the predicted
value from~\eqref{eq:delta-laws} exactly, with one isolated
exception: $\cyc(3) \glue_3 \cyc(3)$, where $g = n_A = n_B$ and the
two seeds coincide entirely. This is the degenerate total-overlap
case (the ``composition'' is actually a single seed), which the
$\delta_{\glue_g}$ formula was not designed to cover; we exclude
$g \ge \min(|V_A|, |V_B|)$ from the formula's domain. All
non-degenerate measurements agree with~\eqref{eq:delta-laws} exactly.

\subsection{The three join cases, intuitively}\label{sec:comp-three-cases}

\paragraph{Disjoint sum: $\delta_{\sqcup} = 0$.}
The two seeds share no vertices and no edges. The multiway
constructions on $G_A$ and $G_B$ never interact: no composition step
$(s_1, s_2, t)$ has $s_1 \in V_A$ and $s_2 \in V_B$, because no
$E_A$ or $E_B$ edge has cross-membership. The gauge equivalence
sorts the two multiways into two non-interacting cluster sets, and
the cluster count is the sum: $|\Qn(A \sqcup B)| = |\Qn(A)| + |\Qn(B)|$.
Under generic ICs, the clusters in the $A$-side multiway are
projectively distinct from those in the $B$-side multiway with
probability one (codimension-$\ge 2$ coincidence), so no
cross-cluster merge occurs.

\paragraph{Bridge by $k$ triples: $\delta_{\bridge_k} = +3k$.}
A bridge triple $(s_1, s_2, t)$ has $s_1 \in V_A$ and $s_2 \in V_B$
(or some mix). Each such triple introduces \emph{three} new
gauge-distinct cluster representatives at the first
composition step: the composition $\mu(\psi(s_1), \psi(s_2))$ is a
genuinely new vector that did not exist in either $M(G_A)$ or
$M(G_B)$, and the three derived edges that follow (Remark~\ref{rem:cache})
contribute two further new pair-compositions, all gauge-distinct
under generic ICs. The $+3$ per bridge is then constant.

\paragraph{Glue $g$ vertices: $\delta_{\glue_g} = -(4g - 3)$.}
Identifying $g$ vertices between $V_A$ and $V_B$ removes their
$A$-side and $B$-side closure orbits' contributions and adds one
shared closure orbit. The accounting is:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
\item For each glued vertex pair, lose one orbit from the $A$-side
($-4$) and one orbit from the $B$-side ($-4$), then add the shared
orbit back ($+4$): net $-4$ per glued vertex.
\item The $g$ glued vertices contribute a single shared vertex
($-(g-1)$ orbit double-count is corrected by the shared orbit).
The arithmetic gives $-(4g - 3)$ rather than $-4g$.
\end{itemize}
This formula is exhibited for $g = 1, 2, 3$ (the verified range) as
$-1, -5, -9$. The $g = 0$ specialisation is the disjoint sum
($\delta = 0$, $-(4 \cdot 0 - 3) = +3$ would not apply; the closed
form is valid for $g \ge 1$ only).

\subsection{The commutative monoid under disjoint sum}\label{sec:comp-monoid}

The disjoint join mode has special structure:

\begin{theorem}[Commutative monoid]\label{thm:monoid}
Let $\mathcal{Q}$ be the set of closure cardinalities $|\Qn(G)|$
arising from Rosen-closed ternary seeds. The disjoint composition
\[
\sqcup : \mathcal{Q} \times \mathcal{Q} \to \mathcal{Q}, \quad
(|\Qn(A)|, |\Qn(B)|) \;\mapsto\; |\Qn(A \sqcup B)|
\]
is the integer addition $(a, b) \mapsto a + b$, which makes
$(\mathcal{Q}, \sqcup, 0)$ a commutative monoid: associative,
commutative, with identity~$0$ (the empty seed).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The composition formula~\eqref{eq:composition-rule} with $\delta_{\sqcup}
= 0$ gives $|\Qn(A \sqcup B)| = |\Qn(A)| + |\Qn(B)|$. Integer addition
is associative and commutative, and $0$ is the identity (the empty
seed has empty multiway and empty gauge quotient, contributing $0$ to
any composition).
\end{proof}

The monoid structure under disjoint sum is the cleanest piece of the
composition algebra. Under the other join modes (bridge, glue), the
composition is associative \emph{up to the interface term}, but the
interface term itself is non-trivially composable across multi-way
joins; the monoid structure is only on $\sqcup$.

\subsection{$\Ccls$-closure as disjoint self-composition}
\label{sec:comp-Cclosure}

The clean $\Ccls$-doubling rule $|\Ccls\Qn| = 2|\Qn|$ of
\S\ref{sec:law-Cdouble} now fits naturally into the composition
algebra:

\begin{proposition}[$\Ccls$ as disjoint self-composition]
\label{prop:C-as-disjoint}
For any $\Qn = \Qn(G; \psi_0)$ in the family under truly-generic ICs,
\[
\Ccls\Qn \;=\; \Qn(G; \psi_0) \;\sqcup\; \Qn(G; \overline{\psi_0}),
\]
where $\sqcup$ is the disjoint composition of
\S\ref{sec:comp-setup}. Consequently $|\Ccls\Qn| = 2 |\Qn|$ via the
disjoint composition rule $\delta_{\sqcup} = 0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By Definition~\ref{def:Cclosure}, $\Ccls\Qn$ is the gauge quotient
of the union $M(G; \psi_0) \cup M(G; \overline{\psi_0})$. Under
truly-generic ICs, $\Jconj$ is fixed-point-free
(Theorem~\ref{thm:J-free}), so no representative of the
$\overline{\psi_0}$-sector is gauge-equivalent to any representative
of the $\psi_0$-sector. The two sectors are therefore structurally
disjoint: the composition is the disjoint sum $\sqcup$, with
$\delta = 0$, and the cardinality is the sum
$|\Qn| + |\Qn| = 2|\Qn|$.
\end{proof}

This identification --- $\Ccls$-closure is a disjoint self-composition
under generic ICs --- is the structural reason the doubling rule is
clean. It also predicts the Pool A defect: when $\Jconj$ acquires
$f$ fixed points (Pool A at $\Kn$ for $n \ge 10$ has $f = 1$), the
two sectors share $f$ clusters, the composition is not strictly
disjoint, and the cardinality is $2|\Qn| - f$, not $2|\Qn|$. The
Pool A defect of \S\ref{sec:ic-PoolA} is the composition algebra
detecting non-disjointness in the IC pool's induced sectors.

\subsection{The algebra is purely additive}\label{sec:comp-additive}

The composition algebra is \emph{purely additive}: the only
combination rule for closure cardinalities is integer addition (with
the interface correction). There is \emph{no} multiplicative
combination rule.

\begin{remark}[No multiplicative rule]\label{rem:no-mult}
A naive guess based on the factor structure of closure cardinalities
would be: $|\Qn(A)| \cdot |\Qn(B)| = |\Qn(A \otimes B)|$ for some
``tensor'' composition $\otimes$. This fails: for example,
$|\Qn(G_0)| = 24$ and $|\Qn(\cyc(3))| = 12$, yet there is no seed
in the canonical family that closes to $24 \cdot 12 = 288$.
Similarly $45 = 9 \cdot 5$ but $Q_{45}$ is \emph{not} a tensor
product of $Q_9$ and $Q_5$: neither $Q_9$ nor $Q_5$ are
generic-regime objects (\S\ref{sec:ic-small}), and no composition
operator we have studied multiplies cardinalities. The factor
structure of the integer $|\Qn|$ is a property of the integer, not
of the closure construction.
\end{remark}

This is a falsification of a natural prior intuition: the closure
algebra is structurally analogous to a vector-space direct sum (with
an interface correction), not a tensor product. Multiplicative
combination would require a different mathematical structure
entirely --- one in which composition of seeds produces a multiway
graph that is the multiplicative-combinatorial product of the
two source multiways. The CFS composition rule
\eqref{eq:mu} does not have this form.

\subsection{Status}\label{sec:comp-status}

Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-delta} (universal $\delta$),
Theorem~\ref{thm:monoid} (commutative monoid under $\sqcup$), and
Proposition~\ref{prop:C-as-disjoint} ($\Ccls$ as disjoint
self-composition) are the three structural results of this section.
The first is established empirically across eight $(A, B)$ pairs and
six join modes (with one degenerate exception, the $\cyc(3) \glue_3
\cyc(3)$ total-overlap case which lies outside the formula's domain).
A closed-form algebraic proof of $\delta_{\sqcup} = 0$ is sketched
in Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}; closed-form proofs of $\delta_{\bridge_k}
= +3k$ and $\delta_{\glue_g} = -(4g - 3)$ are not in this paper.
The second is a direct consequence of the first. The third uses
the second together with Theorem~\ref{thm:J-free}.

\section{Validity Boundary}\label{sec:validity-boundary}

The results of this paper separate into three layers by evidential
strength. Making the boundaries explicit clarifies what is settled
methodology, what is exact computation pending closed-form derivation,
and what is discovered structure awaiting proof.

\subsection{The arithmetic and audit layer (settled)}\label{sec:vb-audit}

The arithmetic-and-audit layer is closed under the methods used here.
Exact Gaussian-integer ray arithmetic
(\S\ref{sec:methods-exact}) replaces fidelity-threshold clustering, so
projective equivalence is tested by exact vanishing of the integer
cross-product rather than by numerical proximity. This directly
resolves the prior $Q_{98}$ artefact as $Q_{102}$, eliminates the
claimed $Q_{180}$ route by $\Ccls$-idempotence
(\S\ref{sec:regrounding}), and exposes the s58/Pool-A genericity defect
(\S\ref{sec:ic-genericity}). These are not interpretive claims; they
are exact-computational corrections under declared validity gates.

\subsection{The closure-law layer (validated, partially derived)}\label{sec:vb-laws}

The closure-law layer is strongly validated but not fully derived. The
cyclic law $\cyc(n) \to 4n$ (\S\ref{sec:law-cyc}) and the small-family
terminality claims (\S\ref{sec:terminality}) have direct structural
explanations. The pentagonal law $\Kn \to n(3n{-}1)/2$
(\S\ref{sec:law-Kn}) is exact across $n = 6..20$ and across both
strict-generic IC pools, but should not be treated as a closed theorem
until a counting proof derives $n(3n{-}1)/2$ from the multiway quotient
structure itself.

\subsection{The composition-algebra layer (validated-conjectural)}\label{sec:vb-algebra}

The composition-algebra layer (\S\ref{sec:composition}) is
discovery-grade rather than theorem-grade. The additive form
\[
  \Qn(A\circ B)=\Qn(A)+\Qn(B)+\delta(\mathrm{interface})
\]
is the correct organising hypothesis for the observed landscape, but
the bridge and glue corrections require independent derivation. Until
then, the algebra is labelled \emph{validated-conjectural}. The
disjoint case $\delta_{\sqcup} = 0$ has a structural proof
(Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}); the bridge ($+3k$) and glue ($-(4g{-}3)$)
corrections remain open (\S\ref{sec:proof-program}).

\subsection{Boundary summary}\label{sec:vb-summary}

This boundary does not weaken the paper; it clarifies the
contribution. The manuscript establishes the exact audit substrate and
exposes the correct quotient landscape, then identifies the algebraic
proof program needed to convert finite exact validation into universal
closure theory. The Galois-pair observation of
\S\ref{sec:galois-pair} below sits in the first
(\emph{settled, exact-computational}) tier: it inherits its evidential
strength from the same exact-arithmetic re-grounding that resolves
$Q_{98} \to Q_{102}$.

\section{A Cyclotomic Galois Pair in
$\mathrm{Spec}(L_1(Q_{51}))$}\label{sec:galois-pair}

We close the body of the paper with an algebraic observation that the
exact-arithmetic reconstruction of $Q_{51}$ makes visible: a
$\bZ_3$-cyclotomic Galois pair in the discrete Hodge $1$-Laplacian
spectrum of $Q_{51}$, and a shared mechanism that places the same
pair in the spectrum of an independently-constructed flat-torus
$2$-complex.

This section reports a CFS-internal spectral consequence of the
exact-arithmetic re-grounding of \S\ref{sec:methods}--\S\ref{sec:regrounding};
it is not a derivation between CFS and any external framework, and
the algebraic observation it reports is independent of the rest of
the paper's claims (i.e. it does not bear on the audit or composition
layers above).

\subsection{The Hodge $1$-Laplacian on $Q_{51}$}\label{sec:galois-L1}

Treat the exact $Q_{51}$ as a $2$-complex $X$ whose $0$-cells are the
$51$ ray-equivalence clusters, $1$-cells are unordered pairs of
clusters appearing in some $q_{he}$ triple, and $2$-cells are the
sorted-unordered triples themselves. Let $d_0\colon C^0 \to C^1$ and
$d_1\colon C^1 \to C^2$ denote the standard simplicial coboundary
maps with alternating signs on sorted faces. The discrete Hodge
$1$-Laplacian
\[
  L_1 \;:=\; d_0\,d_0^{\!\top} \;+\; d_1^{\!\top}\,d_1
\]
acts on $C^1$. With the canonical simplicial chain-space convention
(unordered $1$- and $2$-cells), $\dim C^1 = 315$ and $L_1$ is a
$315 \times 315$ integer-symmetric matrix.

\begin{theorem}[\textbf{Galois pair}]\label{thm:galois-pair}
The spectrum of $L_1$ on the exact $Q_{51}$ contains the conjugate pair
\[
  \{3-\sqrt 3,\; 3+\sqrt 3\}
  \;\subset\; \mathrm{Spec}(L_1(Q_{51})),
\]
the roots of $x^2 - 6x + 6 \in \bZ[x]$, each appearing at multiplicity
$30$ for a total of $60$ dimensions. The pair appears at the same
multiplicity under the alternative ordered-tuple chain-space
convention used by the project's Julia driver (which inflates
$\dim C^1$ to $336$).
\end{theorem}

The eigenvalues are exact at machine zero on the integer matrix; the
characteristic polynomial of $L_1$ lies in $\bZ[x]$, so $\mathrm{Spec}(L_1)$
is Galois-stable over $\bQ$, and the pair must occur with matched
multiplicities under any rational specification of the chain space.

\subsection{$S_6$ action and per-eigenspace decomposition}\label{sec:galois-s6}

The seed $K_6^3$ carries the natural symmetric-group action
$S_6 = \mathrm{Aut}(K_6) \subseteq \mathrm{Aut}(K_6^3)$, which lifts
to a permutation of the $51$ clusters. The lift partitions
$\{0, 1, \dots, 50\}$ into three orbits:
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{0pt}
\item $V_6$ (the six seed clusters, orbit size~$6$);
\item $V_{15}$ (clusters whose seed-co-occurrence vector is a
  permutation of $(11, 11, 4, 4, 4, 4)$, orbit size~$15 = \binom{6}{2}$);
\item $V_{30}$ (clusters whose seed-co-occurrence vector is a
  permutation of $(8, 2, 2, 2, 2, 2)$, orbit size~$30 = 6 \cdot 5$).
\end{itemize}

For each conjugacy-class representative $\sigma \in S_6$, the lifted
vertex permutation $\sigma_V$ extends to a signed edge permutation
$\sigma_E$ on $C^1$ (sign tracking orientation reversal on sorted
edges). The commutation $\sigma_E L_1 = L_1 \sigma_E$ holds at
\emph{machine zero on the integer matrix} for representatives of all
eleven $S_6$ conjugacy classes; see Appendix~\ref{app:galois-tables}
for the character vector and the full decomposition.

\begin{theorem}[\textbf{Per-eigenspace decomposition}]\label{thm:galois-irrep}
For both $\lambda = 3 - \sqrt 3$ and $\lambda = 3 + \sqrt 3$, the
$30$-dimensional $\lambda$-eigenspace of $L_1$ on $Q_{51}$ decomposes
as an $S_6$-module as
\[
  V_\lambda \;=\; [4,2] \;\oplus\; [3,3] \;\oplus\; [3,2,1],
\]
of dimensions $9 + 5 + 16 = 30$. The two eigenspaces carry identical
irrep content (a forced consequence of integer-charpoly
Galois closure: $\Gal(\overline\bQ/\bQ)$ acts by permuting eigenspaces
along Galois orbits while preserving the $\bQ$-rational $S_6$-isotypic
decomposition).
\end{theorem}

The verification of Theorem~\ref{thm:galois-irrep} is by Frobenius
reciprocity applied to the restricted character
$\chi_\lambda(\sigma) = \mathrm{Tr}(P_\lambda \sigma_E P_\lambda)$,
where $P_\lambda$ is the orthogonal projector onto $V_\lambda$. Integer
multiplicities ($m_{\rho} = 1$ for $\rho \in \{[4,2], [3,3], [3,2,1]\}$,
zero for the other eight $S_6$ irreps) come out of the formula and the
sum $9 + 5 + 16 = 30$ matches $\dim V_\lambda$ exactly. The same
calculation is reproduced by the Python verifier
\path{q51_exact_phase_s_python.py}; run output is committed at
\path{q51_exact_phase_s_run.txt}.

\subsection{The $\bZ_3$-cyclotomic source of $\sqrt 3$}\label{sec:galois-cyclotomic}

The irrationality $\sqrt 3 = \omega - \omega^{-1}$ (with $\omega =
e^{2\pi i / 3}$, $\omega + \omega^{-1} = -1$, $\omega \omega^{-1} = 1$)
points at the source: any operator built linearly from coefficients
that are sums of $\omega^k$ ($k \in \{0, 1, 2\}$), evaluated on a
self-dual real-eigenvector subspace, generates eigenvalues in
$\bQ(\sqrt 3)$. In~$L_1(Q_{51})$, the relevant subspaces are exactly the
$\sigma_E$-isotypic pieces whose $S_6$-characters carry $3$-cycle
content with nonzero weight on the classes $(3, 1^3)$ and $(3, 2, 1)$.
The three irreps in~$V_\lambda$ are precisely those: $[4,2]$ has $\chi
= 0$ on $(3, 1^3)$ but nonzero on $(3, 2, 1)$; $[3,3]$ and $[3, 2, 1]$
both carry nonzero $3$-cycle content.

\subsection{The same pair on the flat torus
$T^2_{3,3}$}\label{sec:galois-torus}

The same eigenvalue pair appears in a substrate that has no
multiway-rewriting origin at all. Let $T^2_{3,3}$ denote the
triangulated flat torus on $V = \bZ_3 \times \bZ_3$, with the hexagonal
incidence (six neighbours per vertex; $|E| = 27$, $|F| = 18$,
$\chi = 0 = \chi(T^2)$). Its automorphism group is the semidirect
product $(\bZ_3 \times \bZ_3) \rtimes D_6$ of order $108$. Clifford
theory decomposes $C^1(T^2_{3,3})$ into the eleven irreducible
representations of $\mathrm{Aut}$, and a direct Frobenius-reciprocity
calculation places exactly two copies of the natural $\bZ_3$-cyclotomic
irrep in the $\{3 \mp \sqrt 3\}$-eigenspaces (one copy each, by Galois
closure on the integer charpoly of $L_1(T^2_{3,3})$).

\begin{theorem}[\textbf{Cross-substrate Galois pair}]\label{thm:galois-cross}
The spectra of the discrete Hodge $1$-Laplacian on $Q_{51}$ and on
$T^2_{3,3}$ both contain $\{3 - \sqrt 3,\, 3 + \sqrt 3\}$, with
multiplicities $30$ on $Q_{51}$ and $6$ on $T^2_{3,3}$. The pair
appears in both substrates through the same algebraic mechanism: a
$\bZ_3$-cyclotomic substructure of the automorphism group, lifted to
$L_1$ via the chain-space irrep decomposition.
\end{theorem}

We frame Theorem~\ref{thm:galois-cross} as an observation about a
\emph{sufficient algebraic ingredient} for the pair to appear. Two
contrast cases support the framing: the octahedron with $O_h$
symmetry and the icosahedron with $A_5$ symmetry both fail to host a
$\bZ_3$-cyclotomic factor of the type used here, and neither carries the
$\{3 \mp \sqrt 3\}$ pair in its $L_1$ spectrum (full discussion in
Appendix~\ref{app:cross-substrate}).

The cross-substrate observation is independent of the closure-quotient
landscape proper: it is a downstream algebraic consequence of having
$Q_{51}$ available as an exact-arithmetic $2$-complex with its
$S_6$-action accessible. We list it here both for honest disclosure
(this is the observation that first surfaced the
fidelity-vs-exact distinction during a peer review of an earlier
draft, leading to the cross-repo substrate-provenance discipline
\cite{tce-substrate-provenance}) and because the result it reports
sits cleanly in the audit-tier of the validity boundary above.

\subsection{The discrete Hodge $1$-Laplacian}\label{sec:dh-bg-L1}

Let $X = (V, E, F)$ be an oriented finite simplicial $2$-complex with
$|V| = n_0$, $|E| = n_1$, $|F| = n_2$. Order each cell internally by an
arbitrary fixed total order on $V$; we use sorted-tuple orientation
throughout. The coboundary maps
\begin{equation}
  d_0\colon C^0(X) \to C^1(X), \qquad
  d_1\colon C^1(X) \to C^2(X)
\end{equation}
have matrix entries in $\{0, \pm 1\}$ as follows. For an edge $e = (a, b)$
with $a < b$: $(d_0)_{e, a} = -1$, $(d_0)_{e, b} = +1$, zero elsewhere.
For a face $f = (a, b, c)$ with $a < b < c$: $(d_1)_{f, (a,b)} = +1$,
$(d_1)_{f, (a,c)} = -1$, $(d_1)_{f, (b,c)} = +1$, zero elsewhere. The
relation $d_1 d_0 = 0$ holds entry-by-entry on $\bZ$.

The discrete Hodge $1$-Laplacian is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:L1-defn}
  L_1 \;=\; d_0\,d_0^\top \;+\; d_1^\top\,d_1
  \;\in\; M_{n_1}(\bZ),
\end{equation}
a positive-semidefinite integer-symmetric matrix. The Hodge decomposition
$C^1(X) = \mathrm{im}(d_0) \oplus \ker(L_1) \oplus \mathrm{im}(d_1^\top)$
identifies $\ker(L_1) \cong H^1(X; \bR)$.

\subsection{Automorphism actions and signed edge permutations}\label{sec:dh-bg-sigmaE}

For $\sigma \in \Aut(X)$ a simplicial automorphism (a bijection
$\sigma_V\colon V \to V$ preserving incidence on $E$ and $F$), the
induced action $\sigma_E$ on $C^1(X)$ is a \emph{signed} permutation: for
$e = (a, b)$ with $a < b$, $\sigma_V$ maps to $\{\sigma_V(a), \sigma_V(b)\}$,
and the resulting sorted edge is either $(\sigma_V(a), \sigma_V(b))$
(if $\sigma_V(a) < \sigma_V(b)$, sign~$+1$) or
$(\sigma_V(b), \sigma_V(a))$ (sign~$-1$).

\begin{lemma}[Commutation]\label{lem:commute}
For any $\sigma \in \Aut(X)$, $\sigma_E L_1 = L_1 \sigma_E$ exactly on the
integer matrix.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
$\sigma$ acts on $C^0, C^1, C^2$ compatibly with $d_0, d_1$; the
sign assignments on $\sigma_E$ track exactly the orientation reversals
that $d_0, d_1$ apply to a sorted tuple. Concretely, the signed
$\sigma_E$ is the unique lift of $\sigma_V$ that makes $\sigma_E d_0 =
d_0 \sigma_V$ and $\sigma_F d_1 = d_1 \sigma_E$ commute as integer
matrix equations.
\end{proof}

For each substrate below we verify Lemma~\ref{lem:commute} directly on
the integer $L_1$ for every conjugacy-class representative, with
$\max_{i,j} |(\sigma_E L_1 - L_1 \sigma_E)_{ij}| = 0$ exactly.

\subsection{Frobenius reciprocity and Clifford theory}\label{sec:dh-bg-frobenius}

For a finite group $G$ acting on a representation~$\Pi$ (here, $C^1(X)$
under $\Aut(X)$), the multiplicity of an irreducible representation~$\rho$
is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:frobenius}
  m_\rho \;=\; \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} \overline{\chi_\rho(g)}\,
  \chi_\Pi(g) \;=\; \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_C |C|\, \chi_\rho(C)\, \chi_\Pi(C),
\end{equation}
where the second sum is over conjugacy classes~$C$ of~$G$. For $G$ with
integer-valued characters (true for $A_5$, $S_4$, and $O_h$ on $C^1$ here)
the multiplicities $m_\rho$ are integers; row orthogonality of the
character table verifies $\sum_\rho m_\rho \dim\rho = \dim \Pi$.

For the torus $T^2_{3,3}$, the automorphism group is a semidirect product
$G = N \rtimes H$ where $N = \bZ_3 \times \bZ_3$ is abelian normal and
$H = D_6$ stabilises the origin. Clifford theory~\cite{Serre77} decomposes
$\widehat G$ into induced representations indexed by $H$-orbits on
$\widehat N$. The dual $H$-action on $\widehat N$ is via $h \cdot \chi
= \chi \circ (h^{-1})$, partitioning the $9$ characters of
$\bZ_3 \times \bZ_3$ into three orbits of sizes $\{1, 2, 6\}$ with
stabilisers $D_6$, $D_3 \cong S_3$, and $\bZ_2$ respectively
(\S\ref{sec:torus}). The eleven irreps of $G$ arise as induced
representations $\mathrm{Ind}_{N \rtimes \Stab_H(\chi)}^G(\chi \otimes
\tilde\rho)$ for each orbit-representative $\chi$ and each irrep
$\tilde\rho$ of the stabiliser $\Stab_H(\chi)$.

\section{Rational-spectrum controls: tetrahedron and
octahedron}\label{sec:dh-controls}

The two simplest Platonic substrates serve as controls: both have
all-rational $L_1$ spectra with no cyclotomic factor, fixing the baseline
against which the Galois-pair substrates of
\S\ref{sec:dh-a5-family}--\S\ref{sec:dh-other} are read. For each, the
signed automorphism action $\sigma_E$ commutes with the integer $L_1$ at
machine zero (Lemma~\ref{lem:commute}) and Frobenius reciprocity
\eqref{eq:frobenius} fixes the $C^1$ decomposition.

% =============================================================================
\subsection{Tetrahedron under $S_4$}\label{sec:tet}

\subsubsection{Substrate description}\label{sec:tet-description}

We begin with the smallest substrate as a control. The tetrahedron has
$|V| = 4$, $|E| = 6$, $|F| = 4$, $\chi = +2$. The automorphism group is
$\Aut = S_4$ of order $24$, acting on $V = \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$ by all
permutations.

\subsubsection{Spectrum}\label{sec:tet-spectrum}

Direct computation: $L_1 = d_0 d_0^\top + d_1^\top d_1$ on the
$6 \times 6$ edge space yields the single eigenvalue $\lambda = 4$ at
multiplicity~$6$ (the full dimension). This is the expected result for
the boundary of a tetrahedron, which is contractible (so $\dim \ker L_1 =
0$), and the edge graph is the complete graph $K_4$ with constant degree.

\subsubsection{$S_4$ decomposition of $C^1$}\label{sec:tet-decomp}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:tet-decomp}
On the tetrahedron $C^1 \cong \bR^6$, the $S_4$-representation decomposes
as $C^1 = [3,1] \oplus [2,1,1]$, where $[3,1]$ and $[2,1,1]$ are the
standard and standard-tensor-sign irreps of $S_4$, with dimensions $3$
and $3$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For each $S_4$ conjugacy class representative we compute $\chi_{C^1}(\sigma)
= \mathrm{Tr}(\sigma_E)$ as the count of fixed-with-sign edges. With class
ordering $((1^4), (2,1,1), (2,2), (3,1), (4))$ and class sizes
$(1, 6, 3, 8, 6)$:
\[
  \chi_{C^1} = (6,\, 0,\, -2,\, 0,\, 0).
\]
Applying Frobenius reciprocity \eqref{eq:frobenius} with the standard
$S_4$ character table (Appendix~\ref{app:tables-S4}) gives
$m_{[3,1]} = m_{[2,1,1]} = 1$, all others $0$. The commutation
$\sigma_E L_1 = L_1 \sigma_E$ holds at machine zero on the integer
matrix for all five conjugacy classes.
\end{proof}

\subsubsection{Per-eigenspace analysis}\label{sec:tet-per-cluster}

The tetrahedron has a single eigenvalue $\lambda = 4$ at full
multiplicity~$6$, so the per-eigenspace decomposition coincides with the
global decomposition of Theorem~\ref{thm:tet-decomp}: the entire
$6$-dimensional $\lambda = 4$ eigenspace is $[3,1] \oplus [2,1,1]$.
Forman--Ricci curvature is constant on all $6$ edges (this is the
smallest substrate where edge-transitivity forces $F$-uniformity;
\S\ref{sec:dh-cs-forman}).

\subsection{Octahedron under $O_h$}\label{sec:oct}

\subsubsection{Substrate description}\label{sec:oct-description}

The octahedron has $|V| = 6$, $|E| = 12$, $|F| = 8$, $\chi = +2$. The full
automorphism group $O_h$ has order $48 = |S_4| \times |\bZ_2|$, where the
$\bZ_2$ factor is the antipodal map (inversion through the centre).

\subsubsection{Spectrum}\label{sec:oct-spectrum}

The $L_1$ spectrum on the octahedron is $\Spec(L_1) = \{2, 4, 6\}$ with
multiplicities $(3, 6, 3)$, the eigenvalues identified exactly via the
integer characteristic polynomial. All eigenvalues are rational integers.
There is no Galois pair.

\subsubsection{$O_h$ decomposition of $C^1$}\label{sec:oct-decomp}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:oct-decomp}
On the octahedron, $C^1(X)$ decomposes under $O_h \cong S_4 \times \bZ_2$
as
\[
  C^1 \;=\; [2,2]_+ \oplus [2,1,1]_+ \oplus [1^4]_+
    \oplus [3,1]_- \oplus [2,1,1]_-,
\]
where the subscript indicates the $\bZ_2$-parity (sign under the antipodal
map). Total dimension $2 + 3 + 1 + 3 + 3 = 12 = |E|$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We compute $\chi_{C^1}(\sigma)$ for the $10$ conjugacy-class representatives
of $O_h$ (the $5$ $S_4$ classes, each splitting under the $\bZ_2$ parity).
The class identification uses the face-pair $S_4$ alignment of Phase~$\zeta$:
the $S_4$ part of $O_h$ acts on the $4$ face-pairs (pairs of opposite faces
in the octahedron's $8$-face structure), making the $S_4$ action well-defined
on $C^1$ even before parity. Combining with the parity grading, Frobenius
reciprocity against the $O_h$ character table
(Appendix~\ref{app:tables-Oh}) yields the multiplicities stated. The
commutation $\sigma_E L_1 = L_1 \sigma_E$ holds at machine zero on the
integer matrix for all $10$ classes.
\end{proof}

\section{The icosahedral family under $A_5$}\label{sec:dh-a5-family}

Four substrates share the icosahedral rotation group $A_5$ and, with it,
the quadratic field $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$ in their $L_1$ spectra: the icosahedron
(the worked example, \S\ref{sec:icos}); its cellular dual the dodecahedron,
which reproduces the icosahedral spectrum identically as a Hodge-duality
consistency check (\S\ref{sec:dodec}); the truncated icosahedron, which is
\emph{not} a cellular dual and therefore gives the substantive confirmation
that $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$ is forced by the $A_5$ character ring rather than by
duality (\S\ref{sec:soccerball}); and the real projective plane
$\mathbb{RP}^2 = \mathrm{Icos}/\{\pm 1\}$, the antipodal quotient, which
retains exactly the symmetric half of the icosahedral spectrum
(\S\ref{sec:rp2}). Across all four, $\sigma_E L_1 = L_1 \sigma_E$ holds at
machine zero and the field $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$ is invariant; only the rational
coefficients $a, b$ in the Galois pairs $a \pm b\sqrt 5$ vary with the
cellular structure.

% =============================================================================
\subsection{Icosahedron under $A_5$}\label{sec:icos}

\subsubsection{Substrate description}\label{sec:icos-description}

The icosahedron has $|V| = 12$, $|E| = 30$, $|F| = 20$, $\chi = +2$,
$5$-regular. The rotation group is $A_5$ of order $60$.

\subsubsection{Spectrum}\label{sec:icos-spectrum}

The $L_1$ spectrum on the icosahedron contains $8$ distinct eigenvalues:
\[
  \Spec(L_1(\mathrm{icos.})) = \{3 - \sqrt 5,\; 2,\; 3,\; 5 - \sqrt 5,\;
  3 + \sqrt 5,\; 5,\; 5 + \sqrt 5,\; 6\}.
\]
The two pairs $\{3 \mp \sqrt 5\}$ and $\{5 \mp \sqrt 5\}$ are both
Galois conjugates over $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$.\footnote{Quadratic-irrationality
fixpoints in $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$ produced by iterating a discrete operator
on a continuous or large-finite substrate have a foundational
precedent in von Neumann's 1952 multiplexing
construction~\cite{vonNeumann1952}: the Scheffer-stroke restoring map
$\alpha \mapsto 2\alpha^2 - \alpha^4$ on bundle-excitation
$\alpha\in[0,1]$ has unstable fixpoint $\alpha_0 = (\sqrt 5 - 1)/2$
(the golden-ratio reciprocal). Here the $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$ structure
arises algebraically from $A_5$'s two Galois-conjugate
$3$-dimensional irreps, not from an iterated dynamical system, but
the persistent appearance of $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$ in fixed-point /
eigenvalue problems on icosahedrally-symmetric substrates is a common
thread.} The four singletons $\{2, 3, 5, 6\}$
are rational integers. Multiplicities are: $\{3 \mp \sqrt 5\}$ at $3$
each, $\{5 \mp \sqrt 5\}$ at $3$ each, $\{2, 6\}$ at $5$ each,
$\{3, 5\}$ at $4$ each, summing to $30 = |E|$.

\subsubsection{$A_5$ decomposition of $C^1$}\label{sec:icos-decomp}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:icos-decomp}
On the icosahedron, $C^1(X)$ decomposes under $A_5$ as
\[
  C^1 \;=\; 2 \cdot [3a] \;\oplus\; 2 \cdot [3b] \;\oplus\; 2 \cdot [4]
    \;\oplus\; 2 \cdot [5],
\]
where $[3a]$ and $[3b]$ are the two distinct $3$-dimensional irreps of
$A_5$ (Galois conjugates over $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$), $[4]$ is the unique
$4$-dimensional irrep, $[5]$ is the unique $5$-dimensional irrep, and
the trivial irrep $[1]$ has multiplicity~$0$ (consistent with
$\dim H^1(\mathrm{icos.}) = 0$ but $\dim C^1 = 30 = 2(3 + 3 + 4 + 5)$
fully accounted for).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
With class ordering $(\id, (12)(34), (123), 5a, 5b)$ for $A_5$ (the
five conjugacy classes, with $5a$ and $5b$ the two classes of $5$-cycles,
sizes $1, 15, 20, 12, 12$), the character vector
\[
  \chi_{C^1} = (30,\, -2,\, 0,\, 0,\, 0)
\]
is computed via signed-edge traces. Frobenius reciprocity against the $A_5$
character table (Appendix~\ref{app:tables-A5}) yields the multiplicities
stated. The commutation $\sigma_E L_1 = L_1 \sigma_E$ verifies at machine
zero on the integer matrix for all five conjugacy classes. The
decomposition is the unique multiplicity-pattern compatible with
$\dim C^1 = 30$ and $\dim \ker L_1 = 0$ (the constraint
$H^1(\mathrm{icos.}) = 0$ removes the $[1]$-multiplicity from
consideration).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Galois closure on $A_5$]\label{cor:icos-galois}
Under the action of $\Gal(\overline{\bQ}/\bQ)$ on $\Spec(L_1)$, the irrep
content of $V_\lambda$ matches that of $V_{\sigma\lambda}$ for any
$\sigma \in \Gal$. This is forced by the integer-charpoly Galois closure:
$L_1 \in M_{30}(\bZ)$ has $\bZ$-rational characteristic polynomial, so
$\Spec(L_1)$ is Galois-stable, and the $A_5$-isotypic decomposition is
$\bQ$-rational (signed permutations have entries in $\{0, \pm 1\}$). The
swap $[3a] \leftrightarrow [3b]$ at $\sigma$ is the nontrivial Galois
action on $A_5$'s irreps over $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$.
\end{corollary}

\subsubsection{Spectrum (identical to the icosahedron)}\label{sec:dodec-spectrum}

A direct computation on the $30 \times 30$ integer $L_1$ matrix of the
dodecahedron (with 5-cyclic pentagonal boundary maps) yields
\[
  \Spec(L_1(\text{dodecahedron})) \;=\; \Spec(L_1(\text{icosahedron}))
\]
\emph{identically} on the shared edge space, including all
multiplicities and Galois content: $\{3 - \sqrt 5, 2, 3, 5 - \sqrt 5,
3 + \sqrt 5, 5, 5 + \sqrt 5, 6\}$ with multiplicities $\{3, 5, 4, 3, 3,
4, 3, 5\}$. The $A_5$ Frobenius decomposition is also identical:
$C^1 = 2 \cdot [3a] \oplus 2 \cdot [3b] \oplus 2 \cdot [4] \oplus
2 \cdot [5]$.

\begin{proposition}[Hodge duality on closed 2-manifolds]\label{prop:hodge-duality}
Let $X$ be a triangulated closed orientable 2-manifold and $X^*$ its
cellular dual. Under the natural identification of $1$-cells of $X$
with $1$-cells of $X^*$, the dual boundary operators satisfy
$d_0(X^*) \cong d_1(X)^\top$ and $d_1(X^*) \cong d_0(X)^\top$. Therefore
$L_1(X^*) \cong L_1(X)$ acting on the shared $1$-cell space, and
$\Spec(L_1(X)) = \Spec(L_1(X^*))$ identically.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
The discrete Hodge decomposition $C^1 = \operatorname{im}(d_0) \oplus \ker(L_1)
\oplus \operatorname{im}(d_1^\top)$ has $L_1 = d_0 d_0^\top + d_1^\top d_1$.
Under cellular duality, $\operatorname{im}(d_0)$ on $X$ exchanges roles with
$\operatorname{im}(d_1^\top)$ on $X^*$, while $\ker L_1$ is identified via
$H^1(X; \bR) \cong H^1(X^*; \bR)$ (Poincaré duality on 2-manifolds).
Restricted to each summand, the relevant operator is the same.
\end{proof}

Applied to our case: the dodecahedron is the cellular dual of the
icosahedron, both contractible, so Proposition~\ref{prop:hodge-duality}
forces $\Spec(L_1(\text{dodec})) = \Spec(L_1(\text{icos}))$. The
empirical computation confirms this identity at machine zero on the
integer matrices --- a clean structural-results sanity check. The
\emph{substantive} test of ``A$_5$-character-ring-alone'' as the
mechanism for the $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$ Galois pair is therefore deferred to
a non-Hodge-dual A$_5$ substrate; see \S\ref{sec:soccerball} below for
the truncated icosahedron, which fills that role.

\subsection{Truncated icosahedron (soccer ball) under $A_5$}\label{sec:soccerball}

\subsubsection{Substrate description}\label{sec:soccer-description}

The truncated icosahedron, classically the ``soccer ball'', has
$|V| = 60$, $|E| = 90$, $|F| = 32$ ($12$ pentagonal $+ 20$ hexagonal),
$\chi = +2$. Crucially, it is \emph{not} the
cellular dual of the icosahedron (its dual is the pentakis
dodecahedron, a different $A_5$-symmetric polytope). So
Proposition~\ref{prop:hodge-duality} does \emph{not} apply: any
ℚ($\sqrt 5$)-Galois content found here is a substantive consequence
of $A_5$'s character ring, not of cellular duality.

We construct truncated-icosahedron vertices as: for each icosahedron
edge $e = (i, j)$ with $i < j$, place two new vertices at
$(2/3) p_i + (1/3) p_j$ (near $i$) and $(1/3) p_i + (2/3) p_j$
(near $j$), giving $30 \times 2 = 60$ vertices. Pentagonal edges
($60$ total) form the boundary of the truncation cuts at each
icosahedron vertex; hexagon-hexagon edges ($30$ total) are the
central segments of the icosahedron edges.

\subsubsection{Spectrum and the substantive $A_5$ Galois test}\label{sec:soccer-spectrum}

The $90 \times 90$ integer $L_1$ matrix has $22$ distinct eigenvalues
in $[0.243, 8.392]$, with $\dim \ker L_1 = 0$ (matching
$H^1(S^2) = 0$). The $A_5$ Frobenius decomposition gives
\[
  C^1(\text{soccer ball}) \;=\; 1 \cdot [1] \oplus 5 \cdot [3a]
  \oplus 5 \cdot [3b] \oplus 6 \cdot [4] \oplus 7 \cdot [5],
\]
total $1 + 15 + 15 + 24 + 35 = 90 = |E|$. The equal multiplicities
$m_{[3a]} = m_{[3b]} = 5$ reflect the Galois pairing of the two
$3$-dim irreps under $\sqrt 5 \mapsto -\sqrt 5$.

\begin{observation}[$\bQ(\sqrt 5)$ Galois pairs on the soccer ball]\label{obs:soccer-galois}
The spectrum of $L_1(\text{soccer ball})$ contains two Galois pairs in
$\bQ(\sqrt 5)$:
\[
  \left\{\frac{7 - \sqrt 5}{2},\; \frac{7 + \sqrt 5}{2}\right\}
  \text{ (multiplicity $5$ each)},
  \qquad
  \left\{\frac{9 - \sqrt 5}{2},\; \frac{9 + \sqrt 5}{2}\right\}
  \text{ (multiplicity $3$ each)}.
\]
Both pairs sit in $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$ but with rational coefficients
\emph{different} from the icosahedron's $\{3 \mp \sqrt 5, 5 \mp \sqrt 5\}$.
\end{observation}

This observation is the substantive empirical confirmation that the
$\bQ(\sqrt 5)$ Galois-closure field is indexed by $A_5$'s
$\bZ_5$-cyclotomic factor and is \emph{not} an icosahedron-specific
cellular feature: the soccer ball is the corpus's only non-dual $A_5$
substrate, so its Galois content cannot be a duality artefact
(cf.\ \S\ref{sec:dh-a5-family}).

The trivial-irrep occupant of $m_{[1]} = 1$ in $C^1(\text{soccer ball})$
is \emph{not} a harmonic 1-form (since $H^1 = 0$): it is an
$A_5$-invariant 1-cochain in the coexact image of $d_1^\top$. The
soccer ball is the first contractible substrate in our corpus where
the trivial irrep appears with non-zero multiplicity. The mechanism
involves the hexagonal faces (which the icosahedron lacks), whose
6-cyclic boundary admits an $A_5$-invariant cochain.

\section{The triangulated torus $T^2_{3,3}$: the $\bQ(\sqrt 3)$
cross-substrate match}\label{sec:torus}

This substrate is treated on its own because it is the geometric anchor
of the paper's motivating question (\S\ref{sec:galois-pair}): the same
$\{3 \mp \sqrt 3\}$ Galois pair that appears on the closure-quotient
$Q_{51}$ via $S_6$ reappears here, through an entirely distinct
$\bZ_3$-cyclotomic mechanism in $\Aut = (\bZ_3 \times \bZ_3) \rtimes D_6$
(the cross-comparison is in \S\ref{sec:torus-q51}). As elsewhere,
$\sigma_E L_1 = L_1 \sigma_E$ holds at machine zero and Clifford theory
(\S\ref{sec:dh-bg-frobenius}) supplies the $C^1$ decomposition.

The torus $T^2_{3,3}$ has $|V| = 9$, $|E| = 27$, $|F| = 18$, $\chi = 0$.
The automorphism group is the semidirect product
$\Aut = (\bZ_3 \times \bZ_3) \rtimes D_6$ of order $108$.

\subsection{The semidirect-product structure and $D_6$ action on
$\widehat{\bZ_3 \times \bZ_3}$}\label{sec:torus-semidirect}

The abelian normal subgroup $N = \bZ_3 \times \bZ_3$ acts on $T^2_{3,3}$ by
translations $\tau_{(a,b)}\colon (i, j) \mapsto (i + a, j + b) \pmod 3$. The
stabiliser $H$ of the origin $(0, 0)$ is the $D_6$ of order $12$ acting on
the six edges incident to the origin. The character group
$\widehat N = \widehat{\bZ_3 \times \bZ_3}$ is identified with $\bZ_3 \times
\bZ_3$ itself via $\chi_{(c,d)}(\tau_{(a,b)}) = \omega^{ca + db}$ where
$\omega = e^{2\pi i / 3}$.

The dual $D_6$ action $h \cdot \chi_{(c,d)} = \chi_{M_h^{-\top}(c,d)}$
partitions $\widehat N$ into three orbits:
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{0pt}
\item $\{(0, 0)\}$ (size $1$, stabiliser $D_6$), the trivial character;
\item $\{(1, 1), (2, 2)\}$ (size $2$, stabiliser $D_3 \cong S_3$);
\item the six remaining non-diagonal characters
  $\{(1, 0), (0, 1), (2, 0), (0, 2), (1, 2), (2, 1)\}$
  (size $6$, stabiliser $\bZ_2$).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Clifford decomposition of $C^1$}\label{sec:torus-decomp}

By Clifford theory, the irreducible representations of $\Aut = N \rtimes
D_6$ are indexed by pairs (orbit representative $\chi$, irrep $\tilde\rho$
of $\Stab_H(\chi)$), and the induced irrep has dimension $|\mathcal O|
\cdot \dim \tilde\rho$. Summing across the three orbits:
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{0pt}
\item Orbit $\{(0,0)\}$ (stab $D_6$, dim multiplier $1$): six irreps of
  dimensions $\{1, 1, 1, 1, 2, 2\}$ (the six irreps of $D_6$). Sum of
  $\dim^2$ = $1 + 1 + 1 + 1 + 4 + 4 = 12 = |D_6|$.
\item Orbit $\{(1,1), (2,2)\}$ (stab $D_3 = S_3$, dim multiplier $2$):
  three irreps of dimensions $\{2 \cdot 1, 2 \cdot 1, 2 \cdot 2\} = \{2, 2, 4\}$
  ($D_3$ has irreps of dim $1, 1, 2$). Sum of $\dim^2 = 4 + 4 + 16 = 24
  = |D_3| \cdot 4$.
\item Non-diagonal orbit (size $6$, stab $\bZ_2$, dim multiplier $6$): two
  irreps of dimensions $\{6, 6\}$ ($\bZ_2$ has irreps of dim $1, 1$). Sum
  of $\dim^2 = 36 + 36 = 72 = |\bZ_2| \cdot 36$.
\end{itemize}

The total of $11$ irreps satisfies $\sum_\rho \dim^2 \rho = 12 + 24 + 72 = 108 = |\Aut|$.

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:torus-decomp}
$C^1(T^2_{3,3})$ decomposes under $(\bZ_3 \times \bZ_3) \rtimes D_6$ as
\[
  C^1(T^2_{3,3}) \;=\;
  \underbrace{1 \cdot B_2 \oplus 1 \cdot E_1}_{\text{orbit 1, dim 3}}
  \;\oplus\;
  \underbrace{1 \cdot \widetilde 1 \oplus 1 \cdot \widetilde 2}_{\text{orbit 2, dim 6}}
  \;\oplus\;
  \underbrace{1 \cdot \widehat 1 \oplus 2 \cdot \widehat{\sgn}}_{\text{orbit 3, dim 18}},
\]
where $B_2$ and $E_1$ are the dim-$1$ and dim-$2$ $D_6$ irreps named per
Appendix~\ref{app:tables-D6}; $\widetilde 1$ and $\widetilde 2$ are the
Clifford lifts of the trivial and standard $D_3$ irreps; $\widehat 1$ and
$\widehat{\sgn}$ are the Clifford lifts of the trivial and sign
$\bZ_2$ irreps. The sum is $1 + 2 + 2 + 4 + 6 + 12 = 27 = |E|$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
With Aut's $11$ conjugacy classes enumerated explicitly (the Clifford
derivation $6 + 3 + 2$ from the $D_6$ orbits $\{1, 2, 6\}$ is given in
the GPG-side companion notes; see \cite{tce-substrate-provenance}),
the character vector $\chi_{C^1}$ is computed on each class via the
signed-edge trace of $\sigma_E$. Frobenius reciprocity then yields the
integer multiplicities stated. The commutation $\sigma_E L_1 = L_1 \sigma_E$
verifies at machine zero on the integer matrix for representatives of
all $11$ classes. NULL-3 check $\sum_\rho m_\rho \dim \rho = 27 = |E|$
passes exactly.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Galois pair localization}\label{sec:torus-galois}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:torus-galois}
The Galois pair $\{3 \mp \sqrt 3\}$ on the torus $T^2_{3,3}$ sits
entirely in the orbit-$3$ $\widehat{\sgn}$-isotypic of $C^1$: of the
$2 \cdot 6 = 12$ dimensions of $2 \widehat{\sgn}$, exactly $12$ are in
the union $V_{3-\sqrt 3} \cup V_{3+\sqrt 3}$ (six per eigenvalue).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The orbit-3 characters of $\widehat{\bZ_3 \times \bZ_3}$ are exactly
the six non-diagonal characters, whose values on $N = \bZ_3 \times
\bZ_3$ are non-trivial powers of $\omega = e^{2\pi i / 3}$. The
$\bZ_3$-cyclotomic identities $\omega + \omega^{-1} = -1$ and
$\omega - \omega^{-1} = i\sqrt 3$ (more usefully: $\omega^2 +
\omega + 1 = 0$ implies $(\omega - 1)(\omega^2 - 1) = -3$) push
the eigenvalues of $L_1$ restricted to the $\widehat{\sgn}$-isotypic
into the splitting field $\bQ(\sqrt 3)$. The integer-coefficient
characteristic polynomial of $L_1$ on the $12$-dimensional
$\widehat{\sgn}$-isotypic factors as $(x^2 - 6x + 6)^6$, giving the
Galois pair at multiplicity~$6$ each.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Cross-comparison with the closure-quotient $Q_{51}$}\label{sec:torus-q51}

The same Galois pair $\{3 \mp \sqrt 3\}$ appears in the discrete Hodge
$1$-Laplacian spectrum of the closure-quotient $Q_{51}$ (the CFS
ternary-hypergraph quotient from the $K_6^3$ seed; see Part~B,
\S\ref{sec:galois-pair} above). On $Q_{51}$, the pair has multiplicity $30$
each on a $315$-dimensional $C^1$, decomposing under the natural
$S_6$ action as $[4,2] \oplus [3,3] \oplus [3,2,1]$ on each
eigenspace.

The two substrates are structurally very different ($Q_{51}$ arises from
ternary-hypergraph rewriting; $T^2_{3,3}$ is a planar grid quotient with
fixed diagonal), with different automorphism groups ($S_6$ vs.
$(\bZ_3 \times \bZ_3) \rtimes D_6$), different chain-space dimensions
($315$ vs.\ $27$), and different multiplicities ($30$ vs.\ $6$). Yet
the same Galois pair appears in both spectra, both via a
$\bZ_3$-cyclotomic mechanism: on $Q_{51}$ the $\bZ_3$-cyclotomic content
sits in the $3$-cycle classes of $S_6$ (specifically classes
$(3, 1^3)$ and $(3, 2, 1)$, both carrying non-zero character on the
three irreps $\{[4,2], [3,3], [3,2,1]\}$ that host the pair); on
$T^2_{3,3}$ the $\bZ_3$-cyclotomic content sits in the non-diagonal
orbit of $\widehat{\bZ_3 \times \bZ_3}$ (which evaluates to powers of
$\omega = e^{2\pi i / 3}$).

This is an instance of Finding~1 above: distinct substrates with
$\bZ_3$-cyclotomic factors in their respective $\Aut$ character rings
produce the same Galois pair at substrate-dependent multiplicities.
The two appearances are independent of each other (no derivation between
them), but algebraically traceable to the same source.

X-subdivision diagonals. The heptagonal bipyramid (under $D_7$,
\S\ref{sec:hept}) pushes the pattern past quadratic: its $\bZ_7$-cyclotomic
factor produces two \emph{cubic} Galois orbits in $\Spec(L_1)$, indexed by
$D_7$'s three Galois-conjugate $2$-dimensional irreps.

\subsubsection{Spectrum and the load-bearing kernel-dimension test}\label{sec:klein-spectrum}

We compute $L_1$ on the $T^2_{4,3}$ X-subdivision (a $72 \times 72$
integer matrix), build the signed edge permutation $\sigma_E$
($10$ of $72$ edges carry sign $-1$ under $\sigma$, indicating the
orientation-reversing manifestation), and verify
$\sigma_E L_1 = L_1 \sigma_E$ at machine zero. The
$\sigma$-invariant subspace of $C^1(T^2)$ has dimension exactly $36$
and is identified with $C^1(K^2; \bR)$ under untwisted real
coefficients. Restricting $L_1$ to this subspace gives
$L_1(K^2)$ as a $36 \times 36$ symmetric matrix.

\begin{theorem}[Discrete Hodge theorem on $K^2$]\label{thm:klein-hodge}
$\dim \ker L_1(K^2) = 1$, matching $\dim H^1(K^2; \bR) = 1$ exactly.
\end{theorem}

The $K^2$ spectrum has $21$ distinct eigenvalues ranging over
$[0, 11]$, with rational values $\{0, 2, 3, 4, 8, 11\}$ (multiplicities
$\{1, 1, 1, 5, 3, 1\}$) and $15$ irrational clusters. The irrational
content lies in $\bQ(\sqrt 2)$ extensions arising from the
X-subdivision's $\sqrt 2$-diagonals; an exact Galois characterization
is left to follow-up work.

\subsubsection{$\Aut(K^2) = D_4$ and the kernel-irrep characterization}\label{sec:klein-aut}

The full automorphism group $\Aut(K^2)$ is the centralizer of $\sigma$
in $\Aut(T^2_{4,3} \text{ X-subdivided})$ modulo $\langle \sigma \rangle$.
The X-subdivision admits the extended automorphism group
$(\bZ_4 \times \bZ_3) \rtimes (\bZ_2 \times \bZ_2)$ of order $48$, with
the second factor generated by reflections
$\rho_i\colon (i, j) \mapsto (-i, j)$ and
$\rho_j\colon (i, j) \mapsto (i, -j)$.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:aut-klein}
The centralizer $Z(\sigma)$ in $\Aut(T^2_{4,3} \text{ X-subdiv})$ has
order $16$, and $\Aut(K^2) = Z(\sigma) / \langle \sigma \rangle = D_4$ of
order $8$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
A translation $\tau_{(a, b)}$ commutes with $\sigma = \tau_{(2, 0)} \rho_j$
iff $-b \equiv b \pmod 3$, i.e., $b = 0$. So the translation part of
$Z(\sigma)$ is the $\bZ_4$ subgroup $\langle \tau_x \rangle$. Both
$\rho_i$ and $\rho_j$ commute with $\sigma$ directly. Hence
$Z(\sigma) = \bZ_4 \rtimes (\bZ_2 \times \bZ_2)$ with $\rho_i$ inverting
$\tau_x$ by conjugation. In the quotient by $\langle \sigma \rangle =
\langle \tau_x^2 \rho_j \rangle$, the relation $\tau_x^2 = \rho_j$
absorbs $\rho_j$ as the rotation $\tau_x^2$, leaving
$D_4 = \langle \tau_x, \rho_i \mid \tau_x^4 = \rho_i^2 = (\tau_x \rho_i)^2 = 1 \rangle$.
\end{proof}

Applying Frobenius reciprocity with the $D_4$ character table:

\begin{theorem}[$D_4$ Frobenius decomposition of $C^1(K^2)$]\label{thm:klein-frob}
$C^1(K^2) \;=\; 4 \cdot A_1 \;\oplus\; 5 \cdot A_2 \;\oplus\; 6 \cdot B_1
\;\oplus\; 3 \cdot B_2 \;\oplus\; 9 \cdot E$, summing to
$4 + 5 + 6 + 3 + 18 = 36 = |E|$. All multiplicities non-negative
integers; $\sigma_E L_1 = L_1 \sigma_E$ at machine zero on the integer
$L_1(T^2)$ for all five $D_4$ conjugacy class representatives.
\end{theorem}

\begin{observation}[Harmonic 1-form transforms by the $A_2$ sign character]\label{obs:klein-kernel-irrep}
The unique $1$-dimensional kernel of $L_1(K^2)$ at $\lambda = 0$ sits
in the $A_2$-isotypic. The $A_2$ irrep of $D_4$ is the 1-dim sign
character: trivial on rotations $\{\id, \tau_x, \tau_x^2, \tau_x^3\}$,
$-1$ on reflections $\{\rho_i, \tau_x \rho_i, \tau_x^2 \rho_i,
\tau_x^3 \rho_i\}$. Equivalently, the unique harmonic 1-form on $K^2$ is
invariant under the $\bZ_4$ translation subgroup and flips sign under
each of the four $D_4$ reflections.
\end{observation}

The load-bearing observation Theorem~\ref{thm:klein-hodge} is the
kernel-dimension match. Theorem~\ref{thm:klein-frob} and
Observation~\ref{obs:klein-kernel-irrep} characterise the full
$D_4$-action structure of $L_1(K^2)$ and identify the harmonic 1-form
as the dual cochain to the non-orientability cycle.

$2\cos(2\pi/7)$ over $\bQ$ is $x^3 + x^2 - 2x - 1$, and $D_7$ has
three $2$-dimensional irreducible representations $E_1, E_2, E_3$
whose characters take the values $2\cos(2\pi k / 7)$ for $k = 1, 2, 3$.
If Finding~1 of \S\ref{sec:dh-cs-galois} extends from quadratic to
higher-degree cyclotomic extensions, the $L_1$ spectrum here should
contain a Galois orbit of size $3$ over $\bQ$ with roots in
$\bQ(\cos 2\pi/7)$.

\subsubsection{Spectrum and the load-bearing cubic-orbit test}\label{sec:hept-spectrum}

The $21 \times 21$ integer $L_1$ matrix has $9$ distinct eigenvalues:
\[
  \Spec(L_1(\text{hept. bipyramid})) \;=\; \{0.753,\; 2,\; 2.445,\; 2.753,\;
  3.802,\; 4.445,\; 5.802,\; 7,\; 9\}
\]
with multiplicities $\{2, 1, 2, 4, 2, 4, 4, 1, 1\}$ totalling $21 = |E|$.

\begin{theorem}[Cubic Galois orbits on the heptagonal bipyramid]\label{thm:hept-galois}
The eigenvalues of $L_1(\text{hept. bipyramid})$ split into the
following rational and Galois-orbit content:
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
\item Rational eigenvalues $\{2, 7, 9\}$, isotypic to the
  $1$-dimensional irreps $\{A_2, A_2, A_1\}$ of $D_7$.
\item Cubic Galois orbit~$1$:
  $\{2 - 2\cos(2\pi k/7) : k = 1, 2, 3\} = \{0.753, 2.445, 3.802\}$,
  irreducible minimum polynomial $x^3 - 7x^2 + 14x - 7$ over $\bQ$.
  Multiplicities $\{2, 2, 2\}$, isotypic to $\{E_1, E_2, E_3\}$
  respectively.
\item Cubic Galois orbit~$2$:
  $\{4 - 2\cos(2\pi k/7) : k = 1, 2, 3\} = \{2.753, 4.445, 5.802\}$,
  irreducible minimum polynomial $x^3 - 13x^2 + 54x - 71$ over $\bQ$.
  Multiplicities $\{4, 4, 4\}$, isotypic to $\{E_1, E_2, E_3\}$
  respectively.
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Sketch of the closed form]
Substituting $\lambda = a + b \cdot 2\cos(2\pi k/7)$ into Vieta's
elementary symmetric polynomials and using the identities
$\sum_{k=1}^3 \cos(2\pi k/7) = -1/2$,
$\sum_{k=1}^3 \cos(2\pi k/7)\cos(2\pi \ell/7) = -1/2$ over distinct
pairs, $\prod_{k=1}^3 \cos(2\pi k/7) = 1/8$, one finds
$\sigma_1 = 3a - b$, $\sigma_2 = 3a^2 - 2ab - 2b^2$,
$\sigma_3 = a^3 - a^2 b - 2 a b^2 + b^3$. Setting
$(\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \sigma_3) = (7, 14, 7)$ gives $(a, b) = (2, -1)$;
setting $(\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \sigma_3) = (13, 54, 71)$ gives
$(a, b) = (4, -1)$. The empirical eigenvalues match these closed forms
to machine precision.
\end{proof}

The $D_7$ Frobenius decomposition is
\[
  C^1(\text{hept. bipyramid}) \;=\;
  1 \cdot A_1 \oplus 2 \cdot A_2 \oplus 3 \cdot E_1
  \oplus 3 \cdot E_2 \oplus 3 \cdot E_3,
\]
total $1 + 2 + 6 + 6 + 6 = 21 = |E|$. The equal multiplicities
$m_{E_1} = m_{E_2} = m_{E_3} = 3$ encode the cyclic-pair symmetry of
the three Galois-conjugate $E_i$ irreps under the Galois automorphism
$\cos(2\pi/7) \mapsto \cos(4\pi/7)$.

\begin{remark}
Theorem~\ref{thm:hept-galois} extends the empirical evidence for the
cyclotomic-Galois conjecture (Finding~1) from quadratic to cubic
extensions: $D_7$'s $\bZ_7$-cyclotomic factor produces a degree-$3$
Galois orbit in $\Spec(L_1)$, indexed exactly by $D_7$'s three $E_i$
irreps. The refined conjecture (\S\ref{sec:dh-cs-galois-refined} below)
generalises across extension degree.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Finding 1: Galois irrationality $\leftrightarrow$ cyclotomic
factor in $\Aut(X)$}\label{sec:dh-cs-galois}

The eigenvalues of $L_1$ on substrate $X$ are rational over $\bQ$ if the
character ring of $\Aut(X)$ is $\bQ$-rational (no nontrivial cyclotomic
factor of the relevant type), and contain Galois pairs over
$\bQ(\sqrt d)$ when $\Aut(X)$'s character ring contains a $\bZ_p$-cyclotomic
factor with $p$ chosen so $\sqrt d$ arises from the Galois action on
that factor.

\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{2pt}
\item Tetrahedron, $S_4$: character ring rational; spectrum rational.
\item Octahedron, $O_h$: character ring rational ($O_h = S_4 \times \bZ_2$);
  spectrum rational.
\item Icosahedron, $A_5$: $5$-cycle conjugacy class supplies a
  $\bZ_5$-cyclotomic factor; spectrum contains $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$ pairs.
\item Torus, $(\bZ_3 \times \bZ_3) \rtimes D_6$: non-diagonal orbit on
  $\widehat{\bZ_3 \times \bZ_3}$ supplies a $\bZ_3$-cyclotomic factor;
  spectrum contains a $\bQ(\sqrt 3)$ pair.
\end{itemize}

This is consistent across the original four substrates. \textbf{We
conjecture that the indexing is necessary as well as sufficient}: a
substrate $X$ whose $\Aut$ character ring contains no
$\bZ_p$-cyclotomic factor of the relevant type will have all-rational
$L_1$ spectrum (refined in \S\ref{sec:dh-cs-galois-refined}). The five additional v3
substrates (\S\ref{sec:dodec}--\S\ref{sec:hept}) extend the supporting
evidence base and refine the statement to higher-degree extensions.

\subsection{Finding 1, refined: cyclotomic factor $\to$ Galois orbit
of matching extension degree}\label{sec:dh-cs-galois-refined}

The v3 expansion adds three substrates with the same $A_5$ Aut as the
icosahedron (dodecahedron, $\mathbb{RP}^2$, truncated icosahedron),
one substrate with a $\bZ_7$-cyclotomic Aut factor (heptagonal
bipyramid), and one substrate that tests the topology axis (Klein
bottle). Synthesising:

\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{2pt}
\item All four $A_5$-symmetric substrates (icosahedron, dodecahedron,
  $\mathbb{RP}^2$, truncated icosahedron) carry $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$ Galois
  pairs in their $L_1$ spectra. The dodecahedron has the
  \emph{identical} spectrum to the icosahedron by Hodge duality
  (Prop.~\ref{prop:hodge-duality}). The $\mathbb{RP}^2$ inherits the
  antipodal-symmetric half of the icosahedron's spectrum, including
  the pair $\{3 \mp \sqrt 5\}$ at the same multiplicity. The
  truncated icosahedron has $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$ pairs $\{(7 \mp \sqrt 5)/2,
  (9 \mp \sqrt 5)/2\}$ --- the same field $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$, but with
  \emph{different rational coefficients} $(a, b)$ from the
  icosahedron's pairs.
\item The heptagonal bipyramid (Aut~$= D_7$, character ring carrying
  a $\bZ_7$-cyclotomic factor) produces \emph{cubic Galois orbits} of
  size $3$ in $\Spec(L_1)$, with roots in $\bQ(\cos 2\pi/7)$ --- the
  cubic totally-real maximal subfield of the $7$-th cyclotomic field
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:hept-galois}).
\end{itemize}

These observations refine Finding~1 to a conjectural statement that, on
the corpus tested, appears to generalise across extension degrees:

\begin{remark}[Refined cyclotomic-Galois conjecture]\label{rem:refined-conjecture}
For substrate $X$ with edge-transitive $\Aut(X)$: the Galois
\emph{closure field} of $\Spec(L_1(X))$ over $\bQ$ is indexed by the
cyclotomic factors in $\Aut(X)$'s real character ring. Concretely,
if $\Aut(X)$'s real character ring contains the maximal totally-real
subfield $\bQ(\cos 2\pi/p)$ of the $p$-th cyclotomic field for some
prime $p \geq 5$, then $\Spec(L_1(X))$ contains a Galois orbit of size
$(p - 1)/2$ over $\bQ$ with roots in $\bQ(\cos 2\pi/p)$. The
\emph{specific algebraic values} of the orbit (i.e., the rational
coefficients in any minimum polynomial) depend on the cellular
structure of $X$.
\end{remark}

Empirical evidence base across the v3 corpus:
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{0pt}
\item No relevant cyclotomic factor (tet, oct) $\to$ rational
  spectrum. $\checkmark$
\item $\bZ_5$ factor in $A_5$ (icos, dodec, $\mathbb{RP}^2$,
  soccer ball) $\to$ $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$ Galois pairs (quadratic). $\checkmark$
\item $\bZ_3$ factor in $(\bZ_3 \times \bZ_3) \rtimes D_6$ (torus
  $T^2_{3,3}$) $\to$ $\bQ(\sqrt 3)$ pair (quadratic). $\checkmark$
\item $\bZ_7$ factor in $D_7$ (hept.\ bipyramid) $\to$ $\bQ(\cos 2\pi/7)$
  cubic orbits. $\checkmark$
\end{itemize}

Eight of the nine substrates conform; the Klein bottle's irrational
content is empirically in $\bQ(\sqrt 2)$ from cell-diagonal lengths,
which we have not yet attributed to a cyclotomic factor in
$\Aut(K^2)$. Whether this is a genuine exception or a deferred-
identification is open.

\subsection{Finding 2: Trivial-irrep occupancy
$\leftrightarrow$ $H^1$}\label{sec:dh-cs-trivial}

The trivial irrep of $\Aut(X)$ has multiplicity $0$ in $C^1(X)$ on the
torus $T^2_{3,3}$ but multiplicity $\ge 1$ on each of the three
contractible substrates.

\textbf{Mechanism.} The Hodge decomposition gives
$\ker L_1 \cong H^1(X; \bR)$, and $\Aut(X)$ acts trivially on
$\ker L_1$ when $\Aut$ preserves orientation classes (which it does on
all four substrates here). So $\ker L_1$ is necessarily a copy of the
trivial irrep. On contractible $X$, $\dim H^1 = 0$, so the trivial-irrep
content of $C^1$ is unconstrained by the kernel; it may appear elsewhere
in $C^1$ (and does, in the tetrahedron and octahedron decompositions). On
the torus, $\dim H^1 = 2$ is filled by the harmonic forms (one for each
generator of $H^1(T^2; \bZ) \cong \bZ^2$), but those two harmonic forms
are \emph{not} trivial-irrep-isotypic --- they span a $2$-dimensional
$\Aut$-representation that turns out to be $E_1$ of orbit~1 (the unique
$D_6$ standard irrep at the trivial character).

\subsection{Finding 3: Forman-Ricci uniformity on regular
substrates}\label{sec:dh-cs-forman}

For each substrate, the Forman-Ricci curvature
\[
  F(e) \;=\; \mathrm{deg}(a) + \mathrm{deg}(b)
  \;-\; |\{f \in F : e \subset \partial f\}|
  \;-\; \#(\text{parallel edges through } a, b)
\]
(or a closely-related variant; see \cite{Forman2003}) takes a single value
on all $|E|$ edges. We observed:
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{0pt}
\item Tetrahedron: $F \equiv 0$ on all $6$ edges (the small simplex;
  vertex degree $3$, $2$ faces per edge, with the small-substrate
  reduction by parallel-edge count cancelling vertex degree).
\item Octahedron: $F \equiv +2$ on all $12$ edges (vertex degree $4$,
  $2$ faces per edge, $1$ parallel edge).
\item Icosahedron: $F \equiv +4$ on all $30$ edges
  (\S\ref{sec:icos-spectrum}; vertex degree $5$, $2$ faces per edge).
\item Torus $T^2_{3,3}$: $F \equiv +6$ on all $27$ edges (vertex degree
  $6$, $2$ faces per edge).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Mechanism.} Each of the four substrates is edge-transitive (or
two-transitive) under $\Aut(X)$, so $\Aut$ acts transitively on $E$.
$F$ is an $\Aut$-invariant function on $E$, hence constant on
$\Aut$-orbits, hence constant on all of $E$ when the action is
edge-transitive. We expect $F$-non-uniformity exactly when the
substrate's $\Aut$-action on $E$ has multiple orbits.

\section{Discussion and Open Problems}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Cross-implementation validation of the rewriting kernel}
\label{sec:disc-cross-impl}

The kernel underlying every cardinality, terminality, and Galois result
in the paper has been independently reimplemented in Haskell (see
\S\ref{sec:intro-arity}; package \texttt{cfs-kernel} at
\texttt{haskell/}, \cite{haskell-cfs-kernel}). The port uses no Python
code and no shared infrastructure beyond the canonical
Gaussian-integer initial-condition list. Three things follow.

First, the structural-arity claim of \S\ref{sec:intro-arity} ---
\emph{binary operator inside ternary-hyperedge rewriting} --- is
machine-verifiable: it is the type signature of
\texttt{compose :: Cochain -> Cochain -> Cochain} together with
\texttt{type Hyperedge = (Vertex, Vertex, Vertex)}, checked by GHC
under \texttt{-Wall} without warnings. This is Tier~1 evidence in the
sense of \S\ref{sec:intro-tiers}.

Second, the cardinalities $|Q_{24}|, |Q_{42}|, |Q_{45}|, |Q_{51}|$
(single-side) and $|Q_{48}|, |Q_{84}|, |Q_{90}|, |Q_{102}|$
($\Ccls$-closed) agree exactly between Python and Haskell at depth~$5$
under the canonical IC pool. Two independent implementations producing
identical counts on eight canonical objects rules out a class of
implementation-localised errors that a single-implementation cross-check
cannot.

Third, the structural facts of \S\ref{sec:terminality} and
\S\ref{sec:j-fixed} reproduce: $\Jconj$ is a total, involutive,
fixed-point-free permutation on each $\Ccls$-closed $Q$, and the
F-closure cardinality $|\Fcls^d Q|$ equals $|Q|$ at depths $4, 5, 6$
for the doubly-terminal members $Q_{48}, Q_{84}, Q_{90}, Q_{102}$.

The Haskell port does not cover the Hodge $L_1$ machinery (Part~2) or
the Galois-decomposition drivers (\S\ref{sec:galois-pair}), which
remain Python/Julia-only and are validated through their own
exact-arithmetic gates. The cross-implementation evidence here is
scoped to the rewriting kernel.

\subsection{What the landscape characterises}\label{sec:disc-characterises}

The CFS closure-quotient family is now characterised on several axes.

\emph{Generators}: two regular families ($\cyc(n) \to 4n$, $\Kn \to
n(3n{-}1)/2$) and one irregular route (specific seeds outside the
regular catalogue) cover the family of generic-regime objects.
$Q_{12}$ is the generic floor.

\emph{Closure operators}: the closure-growth endofunctor $\Fcls$ and
the $\Ccls$-closure both have well-understood fixed-point structure
on the canonical $\Ccls$-closed family members. There is no
iterated-doubling family: $Q_{48}$, $Q_{90}$, $Q_{102}$ are doubly
terminal (fixed under both $\Fcls$ and $\Ccls$); larger objects
require larger seeds.

\emph{Doubling}: the clean rule $|\Ccls\Qn| = 2|\Qn|$ is the
specialisation of identity~\eqref{eq:CclosedJ} to the
$\Jconj$-fixed-point-free case ($f = 0$), which holds under
truly-generic ICs. The Pool A defect ($f = 1$ at large $n$) is the
visible deviation of small-$n$-generic ICs from full genericity.

\emph{Composition}: the generative algebra of \S\ref{sec:composition}
(commutative monoid under $\sqcup$ with universal interface
correction) unifies the regular and irregular routes under a single
combinatorial law: $|\Qn(A \circ B)| = |\Qn(A)| + |\Qn(B)| +
\delta(\text{interface})$. The $\Ccls$-doubling rule falls out as the
disjoint-self-composition case.

\emph{Genericity}: IC-genericity is a hierarchy, not a binary. The
canonical s58 IC pool used in every prior CFS computational probe is
small-$n$ generic; truly-generic pools require strict-genericity (every
Gaussian-integer component with nonzero real AND nonzero imaginary
part). The $K_6^3 \to 51$ validity gate is necessary but not
sufficient; the strengthened gate $K_6^3 \to 51 \wedge K_{10}^3 \to
145$ is (empirically) sufficient.

\subsection{Relation to closure-v5 family documentation}

\subsection{Open problems}\label{sec:disc-open}

\paragraph{Pool A defect: which algebraic relation? \emph{(Resolved,
2026-05-21.)}}
The s58 IC pool is non-generic at $\Kn$ for $n \ge 9$ in a specific
quantitative pattern (shortfall sequence $5, 12, 12, 17, 19, 19, 19$;
$\Jconj$-fixed cluster count saturating at $f = 1$ for $n \ge 10$).
A follow-up probe \cite{closure-v5-pool-a-defect} identified the
specific algebraic relation: Pool A's strict-generic ICs at indices
$8$, $9$, $11$ are the three cyclic permutations of the same
Gaussian-integer triple
\[
\{ (2,-1),\; (1,2),\; (3,1) \},
\]
and a counter-test (T7 of the probe) confirms that exactly these three
cyclic shifts, when present alongside the $8$ axis-aligned Pool A ICs,
trigger the defect; Pool A's other four strict-generic ICs (indices
$10$, $12$, $13$, $14$), built from different underlying triples, do
not. The $\Jconj$-fixed cluster that appears at $n = 10$ has
representative $[(1, 8), (-1, -8), (1, 8)] = (1+8i) \cdot (1, -1, 1)$
--- the projective image of the \emph{real} vector $(1, -1, 1)$ scaled
by a Gaussian integer. Real rays in $\bC P^2$ are
self-conjugate-up-to-scale, which is precisely the $\Jconj$-fixed
condition. The cyclic-permutation triple seeds compositions on this
real-ray-shaped equivalence class; Pool A's axis-aligned ICs supply
secondary compositions that populate the class with enough vertex
arrivals to make it $\Jconj$-fixed. Either condition alone is
insufficient (axis-aligned ICs paired with non-cyclic-triple
strict-generic ICs give pentagonal; cyclic-triple ICs with
strict-generic axis-clean ICs do likewise). The two conditions
together activate the defect. \emph{Construction guidance:} any
future Gaussian-integer IC pool intended for CFS computational work
involving $\Kn$ at $n \ge 9$ should reject IC sets containing two or
more cyclic permutations of the same triple, in addition to enforcing
strict-genericity at the component level.

\paragraph{Closed-form proof of the pentagonal law.}
Theorem~\ref{thm:Kn-pent} ($\Kn \to n(3n{-}1)/2$) is established
empirically across $n = 6..20$ across two independent strict-generic
IC pools. A closed-form algebraic proof --- a counting argument
deriving $n(3n{-}1)/2$ from the structure of the $\Kn$ multiway
expansion --- is open. We conjecture the law extends to all $n \ge 6$;
proof would establish ``verified'' as ``proved'' in the
closure-v5 spec sense.

\paragraph{Closed-form proofs of the bridge and glue $\delta$.}
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-delta} ($\delta_{\sqcup} = 0$,
$\delta_{\bridge_k} = +3k$, $\delta_{\glue_g} = -(4g-3)$) is
empirically verified across eight $(A,B)$ pairs and the six listed
join modes. The disjoint case $\delta_{\sqcup} = 0$ admits a clean
structural proof (Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}); the bridge and glue
cases are more subtle and not proved here.

\paragraph{Larger closure objects from non-random irregular seeds.}
The irregular seed exhibiting $Q_{181}$ (\S\ref{sec:primes-Q181}) was
found by random sampling. Are there structurally-distinguished irregular
seeds producing canonical closure cardinalities beyond the pentagonal
sequence and beyond $Q_{181}$? The space of irregular seeds is vast;
we have not catalogued it.

\paragraph{Generic-vs-partial-closure boundary for non-$\Kn$ seeds.}
The IC-genericity hierarchy of \S\ref{sec:ic-genericity} is sharp on
$\Kn$ (Pool A and Pool B/C disagree at $n \ge 9$). The corresponding
boundary on non-$\Kn$ seeds --- irregular hypergraphs, intermediate
densities, mixed cyclic/complete topologies --- is not characterised.
A natural conjecture: every Rosen-closed seed has a critical density
$\rho^\ast(G)$ above which Pool A is non-generic; below it, generic.
We have not measured this.

\subsection{Proof program}\label{sec:proof-program}

The validity boundary of \S\ref{sec:validity-boundary} converts the
paper's open items into a structured proof program. The targets below
correspond, in order, to the lines of
Table~\ref{tab:claim-status-ledger} that currently sit in the
\emph{finite exact validation} or \emph{validated conjectural} tiers.

\begin{enumerate}\setlength{\itemsep}{4pt}
\item \textbf{Pentagonal law.} Derive
  $\Qn(\Kn) = n(3n{-}1)/2$ by classifying all gauge-inequivalent rays
  generated by the complete ternary multiway closure and proving no
  additional projective classes appear after depth stabilization.
\item \textbf{Bridge correction.} Prove that each admissible bridge
  triple contributes exactly three new quotient classes independent
  of the internal structure of the joined seeds. The three-class
  mechanism (one direct mixed composition + two induced
  consistency-edge pair-compositions) is sketched in
  \S\ref{sec:comp-three-cases}; the open task is to show
  interface-independence.
\item \textbf{Glue correction.} Prove that a $g$-vertex glue removes
  exactly $4g - 3$ quotient classes, and identify the overlap
  relation responsible for the constant $+3$ offset (interpreted in
  \S\ref{sec:comp-three-cases} as the residual interface degrees not
  eliminated by the $4g$-style overlap count).
\item \textbf{Universal $\delta$.} Establish that the additive
  composition rule extends to arbitrary admissible join modes beyond
  the three (disjoint / bridge / glue) listed in
  \S\ref{sec:comp-three-cases}, and that the $\delta$ for each is
  interface-determined (depends only on the join type, not on $A$
  or~$B$ individually).
\item \textbf{Genericity hierarchy.} Replace the empirical
  $K_6^3 \to 51 \wedge K_{10}^3 \to 145$ validity gate with intrinsic
  algebraic criteria distinguishing fully-generic, small-$n$-generic,
  and degenerate IC regimes. The Pool-A defect resolution
  (cyclic-permutation-triple + axis-aligned IC interaction, see
  \emph{(Resolved, 2026-05-21)} above) is one structural example;
  the general theory is open.
\item \textbf{Irregular-prime classification.} Determine whether
  prime closure counts are sparse accidents of irregular seeds (the
  $Q_{181}$ hypothesis) or belong to a parameterised seed family with
  a closed-form prime-detection rule.
\end{enumerate}

A closed-form proof of any one of (1)--(3) would move the corresponding
ledger row from \emph{validated conjectural} to
\emph{proved structurally}; a closed-form theory addressing (5) would
do the same for the IC-genericity hierarchy row.

\subsection{Conclusion}\label{sec:disc-conclusion}

This paper establishes the closure-quotient landscape as an
exact-arithmetic object rather than a fidelity-clustering artefact.
The corrected family replaces $Q_{98}$ with $Q_{102}$, removes the
spurious $Q_{180}$, identifies terminal $\Ccls$-closed objects, and
shows that IC-genericity is hierarchical. These results materially
change the prior CFS computational record. A downstream consequence of
the exact-arithmetic re-grounding of $Q_{51}$ is the cyclotomic Galois
pair $\{3-\sqrt 3,\,3+\sqrt 3\}$ in $\mathrm{Spec}(L_1(Q_{51}))$ with
$S_6$-irrep content $[4,2] \oplus [3,3] \oplus [3,2,1]$
(\S\ref{sec:galois-pair}); the same pair appears on the
independently-constructed flat torus $T^2_{3,3}$ via a shared
$\bZ_3$-cyclotomic mechanism.

The generative algebra of \S\ref{sec:composition} is the next layer:
it appears additive, interface-corrected, and seed-driven rather than
multiplicative or iteratively doubled. Its present status is
exact-computational discovery with strong finite validation
(\S\ref{sec:validity-boundary}). The remaining task for that layer is
not more numerical confirmation alone, but closed-form derivation of
the pentagonal law and the bridge/glue interface corrections per the
proof program of \S\ref{sec:proof-program}. Once those are supplied,
the closure-quotient landscape can move from validated computational
algebra to theorem-grade structural algebra.

\section{Proof of $\delta_{\sqcup} = 0$}\label{app:proofs}

The cleanest piece of the composition algebra (Theorem~%
\ref{thm:universal-delta}) has a structural proof. We give it here.

\begin{lemma}[Disjoint composition $\delta = 0$]\label{lem:disjoint-delta}
Let $G_A = (V_A, E_A)$ and $G_B = (V_B, E_B)$ be Rosen-closed
ternary hypergraphs with $V_A \cap V_B = \emptyset$, equipped with
generic ICs $\psi_A$ on $V_A$ and $\psi_B$ on $V_B$. Let
$\psi = \psi_A \sqcup \psi_B$ be the combined IC on $V_A \sqcup V_B$.
Then
\[
|\Qn(G_A \sqcup G_B, \psi)| \;=\; |\Qn(G_A, \psi_A)| + |\Qn(G_B, \psi_B)|.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We claim two things: (i) the multiway $M(G_A \sqcup G_B, \psi)$ is
the disjoint union $M(G_A, \psi_A) \sqcup M(G_B, \psi_B)$ as
\emph{labelled} graphs (each vertex carries its colour decoration);
(ii) the gauge quotient sorts them into the disjoint union of the
two quotient sets with probability $1$ under generic ICs.

\emph{Claim (i): no cross-composition occurs.} The multiway
construction at depth~$d$ takes an existing edge $(s_1, s_2, t)$ with
$s_1, s_2$ already present at depth $\le d$ and produces a new
vertex~$w = \mu(\psi(s_1), \psi(s_2))$ with derived edges. For the
combined graph $G_A \sqcup G_B$, the depth-$0$ edges are exactly
$E_A \sqcup E_B$: every edge has all three vertices in $V_A$ or all
three in $V_B$, since the edge set is the disjoint union and no edge
crosses the partition. By induction on depth: if at depth~$d$ every
edge is entirely within $V_A$ or entirely within $V_B$, then the
composition step preserves this property --- the new vertex is
produced from a pair of vertices on the same side, and the three
derived edges $(w, s_2, t)$, $(w, s_1, t)$, $(w, s_1, s_2)$ all
have $s_1, s_2, t$ on the same side, hence $w$ on that side too.
So $M(G_A \sqcup G_B, \psi)$ is the disjoint union of
$M(G_A, \psi_A)$ and $M(G_B, \psi_B)$ as labelled graphs.

\emph{Claim (ii): no cross-cluster merging under generic ICs.}
Two vertices $v_A \in M(G_A, \psi_A)$ and $v_B \in M(G_B, \psi_B)$
are gauge-equivalent iff $\psi(v_A) \proj \psi(v_B)$, i.e.\ iff their
$\bC^3$-colour vectors span the same complex line. The $A$-side
multiway's vertices have colour vectors in the $\bZi$-orbit of
$\psi_A$ under $\mu$; the $B$-side similarly under $\psi_B$. The
condition that some pair $(v_A, v_B)$ satisfies $\psi(v_A) \proj
\psi(v_B)$ is a real-codimension-$\ge 2$ algebraic condition on the
joint IC space $(\bZi^3)^{V_A} \times (\bZi^3)^{V_B}$. Under
generic ICs (codim~$\ge 2$ condition has measure zero), no cross-pair
satisfies it. So the gauge quotient does not merge any $A$-side
cluster with any $B$-side cluster.

Combining (i) and (ii): the gauge quotient $\Qn(G_A \sqcup G_B,
\psi)$ has cardinality $|\Qn(G_A, \psi_A)| + |\Qn(G_B, \psi_B)|$,
i.e.\ $\delta_{\sqcup} = 0$.
\end{proof}

The bridge ($\delta_{\bridge_k} = +3k$) and glue ($\delta_{\glue_g}
= -(4g - 3)$) cases require finer accounting of which specific
clusters are created or merged at the interface. We have not produced
closed-form structural proofs of these. The empirical evidence
($8$ $(A, B)$ pairs, $6$ join modes, exact agreement across all
non-degenerate cases) is strong but does not constitute a proof in the
spec-status sense; the closed-form structural proofs are flagged as
open in \S\ref{sec:disc-open}.

\section{Cross-substrate contrast cases}\label{app:cross-substrate}

We support Theorem~\ref{thm:galois-cross}'s framing of the cyclotomic
pair as \emph{a sufficient algebraic ingredient} with two negative
controls.

\paragraph{Octahedron under $O_h$.}
The octahedron ($6$ vertices, $12$ edges, $8$ faces, $\chi = 2$) carries
the full octahedral symmetry group $O_h$ of order~$48$, which has no
order-$3$ central element generating a $\bZ_3$-cyclotomic factor in its
character ring. A direct computation of $L_1$ on the octahedron gives
$\mathrm{Spec}(L_1) = \{0, 2, 4, 6\}$ with multiplicities
$(2, 3, 3, 4)$ totalling $12 = |E|$. All eigenvalues are rational;
$\{3 \mp \sqrt 3\}$ is absent. The $C^1$ decomposition under $O_h$ is
$[2,2]_+ \oplus [2,1,1]_+ \oplus [1^4]_+ \oplus [3,1]_- \oplus [2,1,1]_-$
($\pm$ indices labelling parity under the orientation-reversing
sublattice), each multiplicity~$1$.

\paragraph{Icosahedron under $A_5$.}
The icosahedron ($12$ vertices, $30$ edges, $20$ faces, $\chi = 2$,
$5$-regular) carries $A_5$ of order~$60$. Its $L_1$ spectrum contains
two Galois pairs $\{3 \mp \sqrt 5,\, 5 \mp \sqrt 5\}$ (over $\bQ(\sqrt 5)$)
plus four rational eigenvalues $\{2, 3, 5, 6\}$. The irrationalities
are $\sqrt 5$-type, not $\sqrt 3$-type: $A_5$'s character ring carries
a $\bZ_5$-cyclotomic substructure (via the $5$-cycle conjugacy class)
but no $\bZ_3$-cyclotomic factor of the relevant type. The chain-space
decomposition is $C^1 = 2\cdot[3a] \oplus 2\cdot[3b] \oplus 2\cdot[4]
\oplus 2\cdot[5]$, uniquely fixed by the constraint $H^1(\mathrm{icos.})
= 0$. Again $\{3 \mp \sqrt 3\}$ is absent.

\paragraph{Conclusion of contrast.}
The two contrast cases support reading Theorem~\ref{thm:galois-cross}'s
$\bZ_3$-cyclotomic ingredient as a \emph{sufficient} algebraic condition
for the $\{3 \mp \sqrt 3\}$ pair; necessity is open. Specifically, a
substrate whose automorphism group's character ring lacks any
$\bZ_3$-cyclotomic factor of the type identified in \S\ref{sec:galois-pair}
does not host the pair --- but we have not ruled out the possibility
of other algebraic ingredients producing the same conjugate pair via a
different mechanism.

\section{Interpretive perspective: $\Qn$ as Rosen $(M, R)$ closure

\emph{This appendix is an interpretive perspective, deliberately kept
out of the main text.} It is not a mathematical claim. The mathematical
content underlying every row of Table~\ref{tab:functional-roles} below
--- fixed-point properties, cardinality doublings, constant-quotient
counts, sector decompositions --- is established in the body of the
paper by exact-arithmetic probes and is load-bearing independently of
the framing here. What this appendix adds is one \emph{optional} mapping
from those combinatorial facts onto Rosen's $(M, R)$-system vocabulary
(\cite{Rosen1991}). The mapping is suggestive, not entailed: a different
systems-theoretic vocabulary would give a different mapping over the
same witnesses, and nothing downstream depends on which vocabulary one
chooses. We record it because the $(M, R)$ reading has been a useful
organising idea in CFS programme discussions --- the \emph{kernel} we
want to keep is that the canonical quotients sort naturally into
distinct functional roles --- but it is commentary on the algebra, not
a result of it.

The closure laws of \S\ref{sec:closure-laws}, the terminality results
of \S\ref{sec:terminality}, the $\Jconj$-fpf analysis of
\S\ref{sec:j-fixed}, and the composition algebra of
\S\ref{sec:composition} together characterise the canonical
closure-quotient family as a combinatorial object: counts, fixed
points, doublings, and the universal interface correction~$\delta$.
This section gives a complementary \emph{functional-role}

constraint maintenance, causal entailment, scale separation, and an
identity carrier.

We exhibit a per-$Q$ assignment in which each Rosen role maps to a
specific canonical-family member, verify the underlying combinatorial
witness with an exact-arithmetic probe, and conclude with a synthesis
remark identifying the assignment with structural features already
present in the \S\ref{sec:composition} algebra.

\subsection{Boundary self-regulation: $Q_{48}$}\label{sec:fr-boundary}

Under the $(M,R)$ reading, one might call a boundary
``self-regulating'' when the operator that would otherwise enlarge it is
idempotent at the boundary. The load-bearing fact is purely algebraic:
the two relevant operators are $\Fcls$ (closure-growth endofunctor,
Definition~\ref{def:F}) and $\Ccls$ (charge-conjugation closure,
Definition~\ref{def:Cclosure}), and $Q_{48}$ is their smallest joint
fixed point. The probe enumerates the canonical
6-vertex-seed family at $|\Qn| \in \{12, 24, 45, 48, 51, 84, 90, 102\}$
and tabulates $|\Fcls(Q)|$ at depths~$4$ and~$5$ and $|Q \cup
\Ccls(Q)|$ for each. Each single-side member $\{Q_{12}, Q_{24},
Q_{45}, Q_{51}\}$ is $\Fcls$-fixed (depth-stable per
Proposition~\ref{prop:depth-stable}) but fails $\Ccls$-fixity by clean
doubling. The $\Ccls$-closed members $\{Q_{48}, Q_{84}, Q_{90},
Q_{102}\}$ are jointly $\Fcls$-and-$\Ccls$-fixed (
\texttt{probe\_q48\_smallest\_doubly\_fixed\_v1.stdout.log}).

\begin{proposition}[$Q_{48}$ smallest doubly terminal]\label{prop:Q48-smallest}
Among the canonical 6-vertex-seed family, $Q_{48}$ is the
unique smallest member satisfying $|\Fcls(Q)| = |Q|$ AND
$|Q \cup \Ccls(Q)| = |Q|$.
\end{proposition}

A side observation surfaced by the same probe extends
Theorem~\ref{thm:double-terminal}: $Q_{84}$ is also doubly terminal.

\begin{proposition}[Doubly terminal members on 6-vertex seeds]\label{prop:Q84-doubly}
The full set of doubly-terminal canonical-family members on
6-vertex seeds is $\{Q_{48}, Q_{84}, Q_{90}, Q_{102}\}$.
$Q_{84} = \Ccls$-closure of the adjacent-36 seed (the perm-closed
adjacent-triple hypergraph on 6 vertices); a stand-alone verification
using the canonical \texttt{build\_c\_closed\_quotient} from
\texttt{q102\_build\_exact\_v1.py} confirms $|\Fcls(Q_{84})| = 84$ at
depths~$4,5,6,7$ and $|Q_{84} \cup \Ccls(Q_{84})| = 84$
(\texttt{probe\_q84\_doubly\_terminal\_v1.stdout.log}).
\end{proposition}

The four doubly-terminal members form a strict chain by cardinality
($48 < 84 < 90 < 102$) and a partial order by seed inclusion
($\cyc(6) \subset $ adjacent-36 $\subset$ q45-seed $\subset \Kn[6]$).
$Q_{48}$ carries the boundary role as the canonical \emph{smallest}
endogenously-stable object; the larger doubly-terminal members are
boundary objects on richer seeds.

\subsection{Repair / efficient-cause: $Q_{45} \to Q_{90}$}\label{sec:fr-repair}

One can read the $\Ccls$-doubling map through Rosen's notion of a
``repair'' operator (one that restores closure using only internal
structure), but the content is the algebraic fact: $Q \mapsto Q \cup
\Ccls(Q)$ takes a single-side half-closure to a doubly-terminal full
closure using only the intrinsic charge-conjugation involution~$\Jconj$. The operator fires
cleanly under truly-generic ICs ($\Jconj$ fixed-point-free, $f=0$) and
fails completely under degenerate ICs (real-valued: $\overline{\psi} =
\psi$, the conjugate sector collapses onto the original).

\begin{proposition}[$\Ccls$-doubling as Rosen repair, conditional on
IC genericity]\label{prop:repair-conditional}
Under POOL\_A and POOL\_B (truly-generic 6-IC sets), the doubling
$Q_{45} \to Q_{90}$ fires with $f = 0$: $|Q_{45} \cup \Ccls(Q_{45})|
= 90 = 2 \cdot 45$. Under POOL\_REAL (every IC component purely
real-valued Gaussian integer), the same operator collapses:
$|Q_{45} \cup \Ccls(Q_{45})| = 45 = |Q_{45}|$, $f = 45$
(\texttt{probe\_q45\_q90\_c\_doubling\_v1.stdout.log}).
\end{proposition}

The contrast is structural rather than numerical: the same operator,
the same seed, the same depth --- only the IC regime differs. Repair
is internal but \emph{IC-conditional}: a system in the degenerate
regime has no repair operator available.

\subsection{Constraint maintenance: $Q_{12}$ as the generic floor}\label{sec:fr-constraint}

Rosen's constraint-maintenance role is the system's enforcement of
structural constraints that prevent collapse to triviality.
Proposition~\ref{prop:floor-Q12} already establishes that $Q_{12}$ is
the generic floor; the functional-role reading is that IC genericity
is the constraint, $Q_{12}$ is its floor-saturating witness, and
sub-$Q_{12}$ closures index specific IC-genericity violations.

\begin{proposition}[$Q_{12}$ generic floor under three pools]\label{prop:Q12-three-pools}
Across the seeds $\cyc(3)$, $\cyc(4)$, $\cyc(5)$, $\Kn[3]$, $\Kn[4]$:
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
\item POOL\_A and POOL\_B (truly-generic): every closure $\ge 12$.
\item POOL\_REAL (purely real ICs): every closure equals the
POOL\_A/B value (real ICs are $\Jconj$-degenerate but not floor-violating
on the single-side count).
\item POOL\_DEGEN\_PARALLEL (all three IC components projectively parallel):
every closure collapses to $|\Qn| = 1$.
\end{itemize}
\end{proposition}

The probe (\texttt{probe\_q12\_generic\_floor\_v1.stdout.log}) thus
sharpens Proposition~\ref{prop:floor-Q12}: the floor is robust against
the $\Jconj$-degeneracy that breaks $\Ccls$-doubling, but collapses
fully under parallel-IC degeneracy. The two degeneracy classes are
structurally distinct (\S\ref{sec:fr-repair} vs.\
\S\ref{sec:fr-constraint}), and the functional-role assignment makes
the distinction visible: real-IC degeneracy disables \emph{repair} but
not \emph{constraint}; parallel-IC degeneracy disables both.

\subsection{Causal entailment: $Q_{24} = \cyc(6)$}\label{sec:fr-causal}

Rosen's causal-entailment role is the system's internal ordering of
cause-effect relations. The multiway-graph composition rule
$\mu(\psi_1, \psi_2) \to w$ \emph{is} entailment in this sense: $w$
is entailed by $\psi_1$ and $\psi_2$ via the explicit ternary
operation. The seed family fixes the entailment topology.

The $\cyc(n) \to 4n$ law (Proposition~\ref{prop:cyc-law}) gives a
constant slope $|\Qn|/n = 4$: each cycle node contributes exactly four
entailment classes, independent of $n$. The pentagonal $\Kn \to
n(3n{-}1)/2$ law (Theorem~\ref{thm:Kn-pent}) gives a growing slope
$(3n{-}1)/2$: cluster proliferation rather than fixed-width causal
flow.

\begin{proposition}[Causal vs.\ cluster-proliferation slopes]\label{prop:causal-slope}
For $n = 3, \ldots, 10$, $|\Qn(\cyc(n))| = 4n$ holds exactly.
For $n = 3, \ldots, 7$, $|\Qn(\Kn)| = n(3n{-}1)/2$ holds exactly.
Slope contrast: $\cyc$ constant at $4$; $\Kn$ grows $4 \to 5.5 \to 7
\to 8.5 \to 10$ over $n=3..7$
(\texttt{probe\_q24\_cyc6\_composition\_v1.stdout.log}).
\end{proposition}

$Q_{24} = \cyc(6)$ is the cleanest cyclic-causal anchor at the
canonical~$n=6$, matching $\Kn[6] = Q_{51}$'s vertex count. The two
generators occupy the same vertex space but produce structurally
distinct closure trajectories: $Q_{24}$'s multiway saturates at
depth~$2$ with the trajectory $[6, 12, 24, 24, 24, 24]$ across depths
$0..5$; $Q_{51}$'s trajectory grows more slowly through the
non-cyclic combinatorial structure.

\subsection{Scale separation: $Q_{51} = \Kn[6]$}\label{sec:fr-scale}

Rosen's scale-separation role is the property that identity survives
coarse-graining to lower scales. We exhibit three distinct
scale-separation witnesses for $Q_{51}$.

\begin{proposition}[Sector-strip cluster identity]\label{prop:sector-strip}
Under canonical Gaussian ICs (POOL\_A), the $51$ \texttt{orig\_only}
clusters of $Q_{102} = \Ccls\Qn(\Kn[6])$ (those reachable from the
unconjugated IC sector alone) are projectively equivalent to the $51$
clusters of $Q_{51}$ cluster-for-cluster
(\texttt{probe\_q102\_q51\_sector\_strip\_v1.stdout.log}).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Depth-stable $\Kn$ scale family]\label{prop:scale-depth}
For $n \in \{5, 6, 7\}$, $|\Qn(\Kn)|$ at depths~$3, 4, 5$ all equal
the pentagonal value $n(3n{-}1)/2$
(\texttt{probe\_q102\_q51\_sector\_strip\_v1.stdout.log}).
\end{proposition}

The third witness sharpens the Hodge-Laplacian structure under the
charge-conjugation involution. Let $L_1(\Qn)$ be the integer Hodge
Laplacian on the 1-cochain space of the Julia-convention cell complex
on~$\Qn$ (cf.\ Appendix~\ref{app:galois-tables} and
\cite{closure-v5-audit-threebug}'s notational fix on the cell-complex
convention). The involution~$\Jconj$ lifts to the edge space as an
orthogonal involution~$J_e$ that commutes with $L_1(Q_{102})$ exactly,
splitting the 840-dimensional edge space into $V_+ \oplus V_-$ of
dimensions $420$ and $420$.

\begin{proposition}[$J$-symmetric eigenvalue doubling]\label{prop:J-symmetric}
$L_1(Q_{102}) | V_+$ and $L_1(Q_{102}) | V_-$ have identical real
spectra in the range $[1.7268, 67.3094]$ at $10^{-6}$ Float64
precision: every eigenvalue of $L_1(Q_{102})$ appears with
\emph{even} multiplicity under the $J$-block decomposition
(\texttt{probe\_J\_decomp\_v1.stdout.log}).
\end{proposition}

The naive identification ``$L_1(Q_{102}) = 2 \cdot L_1(Q_{51})$''
\emph{fails}: $Q_{102}$'s edge space has $210$ cross-sector edges
absent from $Q_{51}$'s, shifting both $V_+$ and $V_-$ spectra upward
relative to $L_1(Q_{51})$'s range $[0.8584, 51.3438]$. The scale
separation thus operates at the cluster-set level
(Proposition~\ref{prop:sector-strip}) and the depth-family level
(Proposition~\ref{prop:scale-depth}), and is sharpened by a
$J$-symmetric block structure on $L_1$ that is itself a new
structural finding (Proposition~\ref{prop:J-symmetric}).

\subsection{Identity: $Q_{102}$ uniquely}\label{sec:fr-identity}

Rosen's identity is the recoverable structure that persists under
admissible transport. In the canonical family, $Q_{102}$ uniquely
satisfies five separately-verifiable identity criteria.

\begin{theorem}[$Q_{102}$ identity uniqueness]\label{thm:Q102-identity-unique}
Among the canonical-family members $\{Q_{12}, Q_{24}, Q_{45},
Q_{48}, Q_{51}, Q_{84}, Q_{90}, Q_{102}\}$, $Q_{102}$ is the unique
member satisfying all five of:
\begin{enumerate}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
\item[\textnormal{(1)}] $\Fcls$-fixed: $|\Fcls(Q)| = |Q|$.
\item[\textnormal{(2)}] $\Ccls$-fixed: $|Q \cup \Ccls(Q)| = |Q|$.
\item[\textnormal{(3)}] $Q = \Ccls(\Kn[6])$, the $\Ccls$-image of the
validity-gate anchor.
\item[\textnormal{(4)}] Maximal cardinality among 6-vertex-seed
reachable canonical members.
\item[\textnormal{(5)}] Sector-strip recovery: the
\texttt{orig\_only} sector of $Q$ matches $Q_{51}$ cluster-for-cluster.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

The proof is by direct tabulation
(\texttt{probe\_q102\_unique\_doubly\_fixed\_v1.stdout.log}):
each non-$Q_{102}$ member fails at least two of the five criteria,
and $Q_{102}$ satisfies all five. The first two are shared with the
doubly-terminal subset $\{Q_{48}, Q_{84}, Q_{90}, Q_{102}\}$
(Propositions~\ref{prop:Q48-smallest} and \ref{prop:Q84-doubly}); the
remaining three single out $Q_{102}$ specifically.

\subsection{Synthesis: the composition algebra carries the
$(M,R)$-closure structure}\label{sec:fr-synthesis}

The per-$Q$ assignment of \S\S\ref{sec:fr-boundary}--\ref{sec:fr-identity}
is one reading of the data. A complementary reading identifies the
five non-identity closure operators with structural features already
present in the \S\ref{sec:composition} composition algebra:

\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{0pt}
\item \emph{Boundary self-regulation} as the $\Fcls$ and $\Ccls$
joint fixed-point structure (Theorem~\ref{thm:double-terminal} plus
Proposition~\ref{prop:Q84-doubly}).
\item \emph{Repair / efficient cause} as the $\delta_\sqcup = 0$
disjoint-self-composition specialisation that produces
$\Ccls$-doubling (Appendix~\ref{app:proofs}).
\item \emph{Constraint maintenance} as the genericity threshold
implicit in Proposition~\ref{prop:floor-Q12} (the floor is
constraint-saturated; sub-floor is constraint-violating).
\item \emph{Causal entailment} as the seed-graph-determined topology
of multiway entailment (\S\ref{sec:bg-multiway}).
\item \emph{Scale separation} as the conjunction of depth-stability
(Proposition~\ref{prop:depth-stable}), sector-strip identity
(Proposition~\ref{prop:sector-strip}), and $J$-symmetric block
structure (Proposition~\ref{prop:J-symmetric}).
\item \emph{Identity} as the unique five-criterion satisfier
(Theorem~\ref{thm:Q102-identity-unique}).
\end{itemize}

This reading says, roughly: the composition algebra of
\S\ref{sec:composition} \emph{is} an $(M,R)$-closure structure with
$Q_{102}$ as identity carrier. The per-$Q$ assignment of
Table~\ref{tab:functional-roles} is then the answer to ``which
canonical-family member \emph{witnesses} each operator most cleanly''
rather than ``which is uniquely each operator.'' Both readings are
compatible.

\subsection{Forward work: carrier manifolds}\label{sec:fr-carrier}

Beyond the closure-quotient family, the functional-role framework
suggests Lie-group quotients as candidate \emph{carrier manifolds} on
which the $\Qn$ family may act. Specific candidates (drawn from
correspondence with B.\ Crabtree and noted here only as forward work)
include $SU(2) \times SU(2) / \mathrm{diag}\, SU(2)$ (dimension~$3$),
$SO(4)/U(1)$ (dimension~$5$), $SU(3)/(U(1) \times U(1))$
(dimension~$6$), and $Sp(2)/SU(2)$ (dimension~$5$). Each is a
candidate substrate on which a delayed dynamical system could be
projected while preserving selected invariants; whether the
closure-quotient algebra acts on any of these in a structurally
faithful way is an open question.

This forward direction would, if it landed, sharpen ``carrier of the
SM algebra'' from a slogan applied to $Q_{102}$ into a substrate map
$Q_{n} \to G/H$ with verifiable invariance under the dynamical update.
We flag it here as the natural follow-up to the present paper.
